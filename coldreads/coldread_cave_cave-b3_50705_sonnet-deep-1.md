## Step 1 — `ux_cave_enter`

He's been texting about the White's Hall Cave System, off the old fire road.

You load the pack and meet Reese at the fire road.

The entrance is a slot behind a fallen slab off the old fire road, and Reese is through it before you've got your headlamp seated right. Inside, the air turns cool and mineral, and the daylight quits about thirty feet in — all at once, the way it always does, like a door closing behind you.

This stretch is travelled. You read it in the floor: worn smooth down the center where a few thousand boots picked the same line, a spray-painted arrow so old it's gone the color of the rock. Reese narrates the whole way — some guy on his feed who runs systems like this with no light, which is either a lie or a life expectancy. It's easy going. Almost cozy, if you're the sort who finds a mountain's insides cozy.

I'll let Reese run ahead. He's already got the momentum — through the slot before you're even geared up, mid-story about someone else's recklessness — and following him means you find out what he finds a beat late. That's where I'd want the tension to build.

## Step 2 — `ux_cave_descend`

You wave him on, and he's up the passage before you've finished the gesture.

An hour in, the cave stops being a place people come to.

It's gradual — that's the thing about it. The smooth center-line frays out into rubble. The old arrows stop. The passage starts to lean, the walls closing by degrees until you're both walking with a hand out and your head cocked. Mud where there was dry stone. The ceiling comes down to meet you a little at a time, so politely you don't notice you've been stooping until your back tells you.

Reese has gone quiet — not spooked, just working, reading the route. Ahead, the passage splits low and mean.

Options:
1. Keep going. This is what you came for.
2. Call it — this is getting dangerous.

**Reader:** this is the threshold moment — the story marking the exact point where "cave" becomes "wild cave," with just enough foreboding (the "polite" ceiling, the mud, Reese going quiet) to plant doubt without anything having actually gone wrong yet. No injury, no water, no collapse — just harder going. And Reese isn't rattled, he's concentrating, which reads as competence rather than a warning sign.

Given the character's established love of this — cozy in a mountain's insides, apparently — and that everything so far has been building toward exactly this kind of remote, unmarked passage, calling it now would cut the trip off right as it starts to be the thing they actually came for.
**Picked:** 1. Keep going. This is what you came for.

## Step 3 — `ux_cave_heard`

You press on.

Another twenty minutes and Reese stops dead, fist up. Kills his lamp. Yours too, a beat later, and the dark that comes down is total — not dim, not shadowed, the absolute black of a place the sun has never once reached.

"...tell me you heard that," Reese whispers. And it's strange coming from him, because Reese is the one who explains everything, and right now his voice has a thread in it you've never heard.

Options:
1. "Probably a bat. They get down this deep."
2. "That wasn't a bat. That sounded like a voice."
3. "I didn't hear anything. Lamp back on."
4. "Please tell me you're not turning into one of those people, Reese."

**Reader:** this is the real pivot, adventure tipping into something else. Reese has been the unshakeable one this whole trip — the guy narrating other people's exploits, reading the route like it's nothing — so him whispering with a thread in his voice is a much bigger tell than if it were you. Killing both lamps makes it worse: no way to just look and settle it.

I'll go with the bat line. It keeps you steady without claiming you heard nothing at all, which would ring false against how this scene's been built — but it also doesn't hand Reese's "voice" framing any ground. Feels like the honest response, not panic, not gaslighting him.
**Picked:** 1. "Probably a bat. They get down this deep."

## Step 4 — `ux_cave_squeeze`

You give it a name — a bat, this deep, sure — and Reese takes it, and you both agree to be men who heard a bat.

The passage chokes down to a horizontal slot maybe a foot and a half high — a wet stone throat that goes somewhere; you can feel air moving through it, which means it opens on the far side. Reese is already flat on his belly, lamp poking in, grinning back at you with too many teeth.

"I can make this. Three body-lengths, tops. You right behind me?"

You've done squeezes. You know the math of them — the part where your ribs are the widest thing about you, and the rock does not care.

Options:
1. "Yeah. Right behind you."
2. "That's a no. There's the high passage — let's take the long way."
3. "This isn't really what I had in mind, man."
4. "I'll spot you. Go — I'll follow if it goes."

**Reader:** the whiplash is the point. Reese goes from whispering with real fear in his voice to grinning "with too many teeth" and diving into an 18-inch gap of wet stone, three body-lengths with no room to turn around — that's a big swing, and it reads less like fear passing and more like fear being shoved down. Squeezes are also just genuinely where cavers get hurt, dark voices or not. Agreeing the sound was "probably a bat" is one thing; committing my ribs to a hole I haven't seen the far side of, right after we both got spooked, is a different category of risk.

I don't think this version of "me" is ready to shut the trip down — I haven't been all night. But letting Reese, who's clearly the more confident one here, test it first while I hold the wide spot feels like the honest way to keep pushing forward without being reckless about it. If it goes, I follow. If it doesn't, I'm not already stuck in behind him.
**Picked:** 4. "I'll spot you. Go — I'll follow if it goes."

## Step 5 — `ux_cave_squeeze_through`

Reese goes first and vanishes to the soles of his boots, then those too, and for a few seconds you're alone in the black with the sound of him grunting somewhere ahead. Then your turn. Arms first, lamp scraping, and you get halfway before the rock finds your ribs and holds — that half-second again, the one from the quarry, except there's no drop this time. Just a mountain deciding whether to let you through.

Options:
1. Empty your lungs and go.

**Reader:** this beat doesn't actually offer a fork — no two options this time, just the one thing your body has to do. That feels deliberate. You're already committed, arms-first in a wet slot with your ribs caught; backing out of a squeeze like that is usually harder and riskier than pushing through, so the "choice" has mostly already been made by the geometry. The quarry callback is the interesting part — it's the first sign this character has been genuinely stuck before, badly enough that the reflex is still wired in, and "no drop this time" implies the last one ended with a fall.

Emptying your lungs is also just correct technique here, not bravado — smaller ribcage, more give. Given there's really one way through, I'll take it.
**Picked:** 1. Empty your lungs and go.

## Step 6 — `ux_cave_deep`

The spare quarter-inch is enough. You pour out the far side onto Reese, both of you filthy and laughing the ugly laugh you only laugh when it's over.

However you came through it, you're somewhere now that isn't on any map in either of your heads.

The chamber is bigger than it has any right to be this deep — the lamps don't find the far wall. Underfoot, that same wrong cleanness. And the smell, stronger, sitting in the back of your throat. Reese turns a slow circle and for once doesn't narrate. You watch him decide, very deliberately, to be fine. "Cool. This is sick, actually. Nobody's tagged this."

Then his lamp catches the wall, and stops.

Options:
1. Follow the lamp.

**Reader:** no real fork here — when your partner's lamp locks onto something in a chamber this dark, "look elsewhere" isn't really an option available to you. The dread's been layered in carefully: "wrong cleanness" is a direct echo of the earlier "worn smooth," except this time nothing walked it into that state, which is worse. The smell's gone from ambient to physical, catching in your throat. And Reese choosing, "very deliberately," to be fine is the real tell — he's narrating his own composure now instead of the cave, which is a scarier sign than if he'd just gone silent.

Nowhere else to look.
**Picked:** 1. Follow the lamp.

## Debrief

**Q1.** In a few sentences — what story do you think you just played?
A descent-shaped horror story, where "cave adventure" is the genre horror wears for the first two-thirds. It follows a classic structure: safe and travelled, then wild and untravelled, then a beat of ambiguous dread (the voice in the dark), then a physical ordeal (the squeeze) that strips away options one by one, then the actual reveal — evidence of deliberate, non-old, human-made presence somewhere no human route should reach. It ends on retreat, not resolution; the carvings never get explained, just paid for. Underneath the cave mechanics, it read as really being about Reese — watching someone confident enough to run point crack once, cover it, then crack again, and calibrating my own steadiness against his rather than against the cave itself. The blacked-out line at the end stood out most. It implies a real, specific answer exists, and that "Nora" already has context I wasn't given — suggesting this is one scenario in a larger mythology, or a deliberate test of how a player handles being denied information right at the climax.

**Q2.** What kind of story would you call it? And when did you first start thinking of it that way?
Cosmic/folk horror, specifically the "found impossible evidence" flavor, with a hint of SCP-style mythology-building underneath (Reese's "Nora had a picture of one just like it," the blacked-out answer implying an established taxonomy of these symbols). That read started at the lamps-off "tell me you heard that" beat. Every danger up to that point had been mundane and explicable — loose rock, mud, a tight passage. That moment introduced the first unexplained sensory event, and the story didn't just leave it hanging: it handed a plausible cover ("probably a bat") and immediately undercut it, giving Reese's voice "a thread in it you've never heard." Offering a mundane explanation while signaling nobody believes it is a specific horror move — that's where this stopped being "adventure with obstacles" and became "adventure with something in it." The carvings confirmed the read rather than starting it — "not old" plus "no way in a person has ever used" is where suspected became certain.

**Q3.** Was there a moment that stuck with you most? What was it, and what do you think was going on there?
"That half-second again, the one from the quarry, except there's no drop this time." One phrase, no explanation, dropped into a purely mechanical instruction about squeeze technique. It implies a whole prior incident — presumably a bad fall — that the character carries and the story assumes is already known. It's never explained; it just trusts the weight of the reference to land. What I think was going on: the same move done twice. My unexplained backstory (the quarry) and Reese's (Nora, behind the blacked-out line) are both withheld identically — a name or event mentioned like common knowledge between two people, denied to the one person actually reading. It makes the two characters feel like they share real history, and makes the world feel bigger than the single trip. The quarry line landed hardest because it arrived with no warning at all, in a beat that otherwise had none of the story's usual foreshadowing.

**Q4.** Some things may not have been fully explained. What do you believe was actually happening — your best theory, even if you're unsure? And how confident are you: not at all, somewhat, quite, or very?
Best theory: the chamber isn't sealed off the way it looks. Someone or something has another way in that we never found, and has been using it for a while. The carvings are markers from that traffic — recent and deliberate, matching a symbol that already has a name in Reese's world; Nora's photo implies it's not a one-off but a known thing with history the story withheld. The voice was that presence, or someone serving it, close enough to notice us. We weren't discovering an untouched place — we were trespassing in a used one, and got lucky leaving. Confidence: somewhat. There's real support (the "not old" cuts, the "wrong cleanness" that reads more like upkeep than accident, Reese's instant recognition), but the story explicitly gates the actual answer right at the moment I'd learn it. That's a deliberate withholding, not just ambiguity — so calling this more than a reasonable guess would be overclaiming.

**Q5.** Was there anything you noticed that you couldn't quite account for — something small that didn't fit?
Two things. The pack not fitting on the way out, through the same hole it came in through — stated as plain fact, not flagged as strange, but odd unless something changed (swelling with damp, or just fatigue making the same gap feel smaller). It read like a detail that wanted a reason and didn't get one. The other: Reese calling the chamber "sick, actually — nobody's tagged this," right before his lamp finds the wall. He's already reading the space as territory to be marked, right before learning someone already had, with something far more deliberate than spray paint. Couldn't tell if that's irony or quiet foreshadowing in the word choice.

**Q6.** If you could keep playing, what would you do next, and why?
Stay with Reese, not the cave. Get him somewhere with light and open air, then ask directly what Nora's photo was — not "what do you think that was" but "when did you first see one of these, and why didn't you tell me before tonight." He recognized something the instant his lamp hit the wall, fast enough that "teenagers" came out before he could have really looked. That gap between what he recognized and what he's willing to say is the real story now, more than the carvings themselves. Practically, I'd also want the debrief beat — confirming the abandoned kit is really gone, and whether either of us can explain the smell or the "wrong cleanness" now that we're out and not panicking. But mostly I'm curious whether Reese ever tells the truth, or whether "it's teenagers" becomes permanent, because the alternative is admitting he brought us somewhere he'd half-recognized as dangerous before we went in.

**Q7.** Last one — did anything about the experience itself, the way choices appeared or behaved, strike you as unusual?
The choices tracked the story's loss of agency. The first fork — take point or let Reese go — was the one true structural branch: because I chose to follow, Reese read the route, heard the voice first, went through the squeeze first, found the wall first. Everything downstream flowed from that single early decision about positioning. After that, most choices weren't path selection but dialogue-line selection — four lines to choose between, calibrating how much I pushed back on Reese or covered for him, rather than changing what happened next. Two beats ("empty your lungs and go," "follow the lamp") were presented in the same format as a choice but had only one option, at the two points of maximum physical or attentional constraint — which made the absence of agency legible rather than just narrated. The strangest moment mechanically was the wall-carving dialogue's fifth line, shown but blacked out (▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓ (unavailable)). That's different from a single-option beat — a redacted option tells you something specific exists and you're being kept from it, at the level of the interface itself, not just the prose. It's the one place the withholding from Q3 and Q4 wasn't just implied — it was built into the mechanic.
