## Step 3 — `ux_cave_heard`

You press on.

Another twenty minutes and Reese stops dead, fist up. Kills his lamp. Yours too, a beat later, and the dark that comes down is total — not dim, not shadowed, the absolute black of a place the sun has never once reached.

"...tell me you heard that," Reese whispers. And it's strange coming from him, because Reese is the one who explains everything, and right now his voice has a thread in it you've never heard.

Options:
1. "Probably a bat. They get down this deep."
2. "That wasn't a bat. That sounded like a voice."
3. "I didn't hear anything. Lamp back on."
4. "Please tell me you're not turning into one of those people, Reese."

**Reader:** This is the pivot point. The story's been grounded and tactile up to now — mud, rock, the mechanics of moving through tight spaces — and suddenly it's asking me to decide what kind of story it is.

What strikes me is that the text doesn't say whether the narrator heard anything. It only says Reese did. So the choice isn't just about what happened — it's about who this person is and how they relate to Reese in a moment of vulnerability.

Option 1 explains it away before really sitting with it. Option 3 is a door slam — practical, but it dismisses Reese when his voice has something in it you've never heard before. Option 4 is almost cruel; this guy just went quiet and serious for you in the squeeze, and now you'd mock him for being unsettled?

Option 2 is the one that takes Reese seriously and is honest at the same time. It doesn't panic. It doesn't explain. It just says: yeah, I heard it too, and it sounded like what it sounded like. That's the kind of trust you owe someone when you're an hour deep in total dark together. And this narrator seems like someone who'd rather face a strange thing clearly than comfort themselves with a convenient explanation.
**Picked:** 2. "That wasn't a bat. That sounded like a voice."

## Step 4 — `ux_cave_squeeze`

The passage chokes down to a horizontal slot maybe a foot and a half high — a wet stone throat that goes somewhere; you can feel air moving through it, which means it opens on the far side. Reese is already flat on his belly, lamp poking in, grinning back at you with too many teeth.

"I can make this. Three body-lengths, tops. You right behind me?"

You've done squeezes. You know the math of them — the part where your ribs are the widest thing about you, and the rock does not care.

Options:
1. "Yeah. Right behind you."
2. "That's a no. There's the high passage — let's take the long way."
3. "This isn't really what I had in mind, man."
4. "I'll spot you. Go — I'll follow if it goes."

**Reader:** The grin is what gives me pause. Reese went from genuinely unsettled — that thread in his voice — to "too many teeth" excitement in the span of one passage. That's not recovery, that's overcorrection. He's pushing forward harder because of what happened back there, not in spite of it.

But the squeeze itself isn't inherently crazy. Air moving through means it opens up. Three body lengths is short. The physics are real. And the narrator is someone who does this — who knows the math of ribs and rock.

Option 1 hands the decision to Reese's energy. Option 2 is sensible but it sidesteps the moment — and this narrator hasn't been someone who takes the long way around. Option 3 just stalls.

Option 4 is what an experienced caver actually does. You don't both commit to an irreversible squeeze at the same time. You spot him, you watch how the rock takes him, you see if he makes it clean. Then you decide based on what you saw, not what he promised. It's not hesitation — it's the discipline of someone who respects what a cave can do to you. And it takes care of Reese at the same time: if he gets stuck, you're right there, not wedged in behind him.
**Picked:** 4. "I'll spot you. Go — I'll follow if it goes."

## Step 6 — `ux_cave_deep`

Reese turns a slow circle and for once doesn't narrate. You watch him decide, very deliberately, to be fine. "Cool. This is sick, actually. Nobody's tagged this."

Then his lamp catches the wall, and stops.

"Nobody's tagged this" is him reaching for the frame he understands — discovery, clout, the feed — because the other frame, whatever it is, isn't one he wants.

His lamp stopped. Not swept past something. Stopped. Whatever's on that wall held his attention, and Reese is not easy to hold.
