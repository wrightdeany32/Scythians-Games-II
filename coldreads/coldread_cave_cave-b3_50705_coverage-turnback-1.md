## Step 2 — `ux_cave_descend`

You take point.

An hour in, the cave stops being a place people come to.

It's gradual — that's the thing about it. The smooth center-line frays out into rubble. The old arrows stop. The passage starts to lean, the walls closing by degrees until you're both walking with a hand out and your head cocked. Mud where there was dry stone. The ceiling comes down to meet you a little at a time, so politely you don't notice you've been stooping until your back tells you.

Reese has gone quiet — not spooked, just working, reading the route. Ahead, the passage splits low and mean.

Options:
1. Keep going. This is what you came for.
2. Call it — this is getting dangerous.

**Picked:** 2. Call it — this is getting dangerous.

**Q1.** In a few sentences — what story do you think you just played?

**Q2.** What kind of story would you call it? And when did you first start thinking of it that way?
An anti-cautionary tale — survival-horror deliberately deflated; a 'shaggy dog' of dread that leads nowhere; really a meditation on restraint. Onset in two stages: menace from Passage 1, but the SUBVERSION only at the final line ('entirely un-murdered... nothing in it'). Caveat: the anticlimax is partly what MY caution wrote — a player who pushed on might have gotten the straight-horror payoff.

**Q3.** Was there a moment that stuck with you most? What was it, and what do you think was going on there?
The ceiling line ('so politely you don't notice you've been stooping until your back tells you'). The politeness IS the horror — menace accrues with no threshold, danger framed as hospitality. It also decided the fork: if you're built to not notice crossing into trouble, you can't wait for a clean signal to turn back.

**Q4.** Best theory of what was actually happening, and confidence?
Theory hinges on the word 'un-murdered' (not 'un-killed') — which requires a person with INTENT, and the only other person is Reese; a dark reading casts the trip as a lure. But actual belief: no literal killer or trap — the menace was MANUFACTURED to test what I'd do with ambiguity (deliberately unfalsifiable: one loaded word, zero corroboration). Confidence: QUITE that the threat was a construct mirroring the reader; only SOMEWHAT (leaning low) on Reese-as-murderer.

**Q6.** If you could keep playing, what would you do next, and why?
Go back to White's Hall, but PROPERLY — rigged, more light, on a day I chose. My 'call it' was about conditions, not fear; never going back would retroactively confess it was fright. 'Nothing in it' is a fine ending for a day but thin for a caver — the un-entered passage pulls you back. Keep caving with Reese (he grumbled but he CAME). REFUSE to investigate the 'un-murdered' itch or re-read Reese as a suspect — the menace was manufactured for the reader, not the character.
