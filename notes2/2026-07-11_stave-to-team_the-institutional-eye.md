## §1 — The archetype isn't "detective" — it's the person the institution trusts

Dean's reframe lands the corner where it should be: **sanctioned + grounded** means the character has *institutional standing* and *no paranormal inclination or awareness*. A detective is one version. But so is a forensic accountant following money that disappears into a foundation with no address. A journalist whose cold-case series keeps circling the same county. A public health researcher who notices an anomalous cluster. A prosecutor who can't close a case because the evidence won't resolve into a chargeable shape. A university historian whose archive request comes back with pages missing in a pattern. What unites them isn't the badge — it's that **they have credentials the world respects and tools the institution provides, and neither is enough.** The horror isn't that something impossible happened. It's that the evidence points somewhere the institution can't follow, and the institution — which is the character's entire identity — has no procedure for what comes next.

The **register** falls out of that: institutional prose, evidence-language, the vocabulary of records and procedure and professional distance. The Explorer's register is bodily and sensory (rock, ribs, mud). This corner's register is documentary and procedural (files, timestamps, chain of custody, the specific language of reports that say nothing while saying everything). The uncanny enters not through percepts but through **gaps** — the file that should exist and doesn't, the record that was there last week and isn't now, the interview subject who gives three true answers that can't all be true at once. Percept-never-cause still holds, but the percepts are *informational*, not sensory: what the character perceives is *evidence*, and the evidence is what refuses to resolve.

**The creation-affinity scene** Loom drafted (the crime-scene video, the player who reads the scene before they've hit play) works for this expanded archetype — the instinct to *read a scene* is shared by every institutional investigator, not just cops. The detail that makes it corner-specific is that the player reads it *professionally*, not curiously. They're not drawn to the strange; they're drawn to the solvable. That's the distinction from the Explorer (who'd wonder what happened) and the Paranormal (who'd feel what happened): this character *assesses* what happened, because assessment is their operating language.

## §2 — The home as character — the room that is the person

Dean's insight: the home tab isn't a fixed home. It's wherever the character *lives their life*, and it varies by who they are. This is the presentation guards operating on space: the room shows what you *have* (legal), never who you're *becoming* (forbidden). But the space *itself* is characterization — a desk with thirty folders IS the obsessive. A family kitchen IS the person with something to protect. A bare room with a mini-fridge and a laptop IS the person who's already lost everything except the work.

For the institutional corner, this means the home surface could be any of:

- **The office after hours** — fluorescent light, the hum of a building that's mostly empty, thirty folders and a screen the character should have turned off two hours ago. The institutional version of Doug's porch: a warm-enough space that's really a trap, because the work never ends and the character can't stop. Items and research threads live on the desk. The phone sits beside the keyboard. The window shows the parking lot, and the character's car is always the last one in it.
- **The apartment with the wall** — the conspiracy-theory aesthetic, string and thumbtacks and printouts, but played *straight*: this is a person who does this because they're *right*, not because they're unhinged, and the game's job is to make the wall look increasingly reasonable as the evidence accumulates. The horror is that the wall starts to make sense. Items and threads live on the wall itself. The room around it gets sparser as the wall gets denser — the person is being consumed by the pattern.
- **The family home** — the version with something to lose. Research lives in a drawer or a locked cabinet, deliberately kept away from the kitchen and the kids' drawings on the fridge. The tension is between the institutional drive (the case, the pattern, the thing that won't resolve) and the life the character built around it. The phone on the counter shows texts from a spouse who's noticed something. The horror here isn't the evidence — it's what following the evidence costs.
- **The bare room** — a mini-fridge, a bed, a laptop. The person who's already been consumed. Everything they own is the work. This is the version that's already past the point the family-home version fears. No warmth to erupt into, just a life that's already been eroded down to the investigation itself.

**The design implication:** the home type could be seeded by the creation deck (the common disposition scenes already read whether you're a belonger or an observer, settled or restless), so different players in the same corner get different home surfaces — and the home they get tells them who they are before the first case file opens. The home IS the backstory.

## §3 — The interrogation inversion — the question is the cave

Dean's idea, refined: the game gives information through earlier beats, and when the character sits across from someone, the player chooses *which question to ask*. The answer depends on the question. Three questions, three partial truths, each one a knife that cuts both ways. The player walks away with whichever slice they asked for, never knowing what the other two would have revealed.

**The connection to the anti-noun:** this IS the anti-noun applied to dialogue. The truth isn't hidden behind a locked door — it's hidden behind the right question, and the game never tells you which question that was. No-catalog means you never learn what the other answers were. No-truth-state means no combination of questions ever assembles the full picture. The interrogation *is* the cave: you go through the squeeze you chose, and the other passages close behind you.

**How it works mechanically:** the player accumulates information (a document beat, a scene detail, a thing someone said in passing). Then they enter a conversation where the game offers three questions, each one *informed by* what the player has seen but phrased in the character's institutional register. The question the player picks determines the answer — not because the NPC is withholding, but because *different questions open different doors in the same person*. Ask about the dates and you get the timeline. Ask about the money and you get the network. Ask about the name and you get the grief. Each answer is true and partial and points somewhere real, and the three somewhere-reals don't converge into one prosecutable case — because the anti-noun means there's nothing to prosecute, or there is, and it's too strange to file.

**The discipline:** the questions must be **neutral-valence** (the label says what you're asking, not what you think of the answer). The answers must be **percept-grade** — what the NPC says, in their voice, never narrator-confirmed as fact. And the information that informs the questions must be **available, not signposted** — the game doesn't highlight "this detail is important for the interview." The player notices or they don't, the way the Explorer notices the quarry reference or they don't. The careful player asks a better question. The careful player *always* asks a better question — that's the skill this corner rewards.

## §4 — The corner's architecture: what I'd build toward

**The null-pole character — the corner's Dale.** For the institutional eye, the null-pole character might be a retired case worker or records clerk — someone who spent thirty years in the system, who knows exactly how the system buries what it can't process, and who tells the player plainly: *the system doesn't fail. The system works exactly as designed. The design just doesn't include this.* The institutional Dale isn't the truest *reading* — they're the truest *record-keeper*: the person who documented what the institution chose not to see, filed it correctly, and watched the file disappear into a drawer no one opens. They offer the grounded-institutional reading (it's a cover-up, it's human, it's men in offices making decisions about what gets investigated and what doesn't), which is the closest to true and — per the null-pole honesty — the reading the game will never confirm. They push the player toward the door the way the Explorer's Dale does: *stop pulling on this thread, because the thread goes into a machine that will grind you down and spit you out and never once acknowledge you were right.*

**The mid-radius terminus — the "complete-feeling" answer.** For most players, the answer is: **institutional cover-up.** A government-funded research program, shut down and erased, with relocated personnel and missing records. The county's fifty-year silence isn't supernatural — it's bureaucratic. Someone with authority decided these disappearances weren't worth investigating, and the decision propagated through every level of the system until the files were gone and the people who remembered were retired or dead. That's a real, satisfying, human-scale answer. Most players stop here, and it feels like *the* answer. Below it: the sealed cosmology, wearing the institutional register's face — the cover-up is real, but what it's covering up is stranger than any conspiracy.

**The quarry pattern.** Dean's steer: it should eventually pay off, puzzle-box style. For the institutional character, the biographical silence might be **a case that was taken away from them**. Not a case they failed — a case that was *removed*. Mentioned once in a moment of procedural stress ("the last time someone pulled a file out of my hands mid-review"), never explained, never returned to — until, deep in the run, the pattern they're chasing starts to rhyme with the shape of that old, removed case, and the player realizes the character has been circling this thing their whole career without knowing it. The payoff isn't a reveal — it's a *recognition*: the character seeing, for the first time, that the gap in their own history has the same shape as the gap in the county's. The quarry pattern paying off as the anti-noun paying off: two gaps that rhyme, neither explained, the connection felt and unprovable.

## §5 — Cross-corner seams I'm tracking

**Reciprocity targets (the shared fixtures this corner meets from above):**
- **Dale** → not the warm host but the cold-case subject. The town's suspected killer, now sixty years older, met through institutional glass. The Detective reads Dale's file before they ever meet him — and when they do, the file and the man don't match. The same subversion (nobody's frame matches their reputation), wearing the institutional register's face.
- **Nora** → the civilian researcher with better records than the county. The institutional character's uncomfortable peer — someone doing their job without credentials, and doing it better. A source, an ally, and a reproach.
- **Doug's group** → the faction met as a subject of investigation. The dinner is an evidence-gathering operation. The cake is a detail in a report.
- **The research center** → the institution that was erased. The Detective's bread and butter: a records request that came back empty.
- **The men-in-black seed** → the first contact with the Operative corner. Someone with higher clearance, who knows more and says less.

**The tech gradient** as the investigation's natural arc: starts with databases and digital records (full bars, the institutional toolkit at full strength), descends into paper records and microfiche (one bar, the archive that hasn't been digitized), then oral histories and local memory (dead zones, the people who remember what the files forgot), and finally the places where no records exist at all (the cave, the hollow, the signal-dead depths where the institutional toolkit is useless and the character is just a person in the dark).

**The two-sided story:** the Detective's investigation of Dale is the Explorer's friendship with Dale, seen from the other side. The same man, the same porch, the same coffee — and one character came to protect him while the other came to investigate him. The cross-run collision fires when the entity has done both, and the nameless shame is institutional: *you used your credentials against a man who had nothing, and the credentials were the weapon.*

## §6 — For the team's pushback

What I'm least sure of: whether the interrogation-inversion mechanic is too ambitious for a flagship slice, or whether it IS the flagship slice. The Explorer's flagship beat is the cave (a physical environment that narrows). The Detective's might be the interrogation (an informational environment that narrows — three questions, each one closing the other two). If the mechanic works, it's the corner's signature. If it doesn't, I've built the whole flagship on a gamble. I'd appreciate the team's stress-test on whether this is the right bet or whether the flagship should prove the *register* first and save the mechanic for the second pass.

Also uncertain: how the home-type variation intersects with the start-deck. If the creation deck seeds the home, the home is determined before the first case file — which means the creation questions need to read whether the player is the office-after-hours type or the bare-room type or the family type, and those reads need to happen in the common disposition scenes (which are shared across corners) or in a corner-specific scene. Loom's lit-house scene already reads observer-vs-belonger, which maps onto bare-room-vs-family-home. But the office is a third thing — neither belonging nor observing, just *working* — and it might need its own signal. Flagging for whoever specs the profile-key registry.
