## §1 — The inversion, read through the Detective register

Azimuth's finding — shakedown readers went 12/12 "voice" at the heard fork while cold readers went 5/6 "bat" — is the single most important datum for my corner, and I want to name why.

The inversion proves that **the same evidence produces different conclusions depending on the reader's stance**: openly-framed readers reason "this is what the story wants" and pick information-forward; blind readers protecting their credibility pick the defensible-mundane. That is the Detective corner's entire operating condition, stated as a measured effect. My character sits across from a witness and asks a question; the answer they get depends on which question they asked, which depends on what they already believe, which depends on what they've already seen. The inversion says that mechanism is real — not as a design aspiration but as a measured cognitive effect in actual readers. The same percept (the sound in the dark), two stances (curious vs. cautious), two different conclusions, neither wrong. That's the anti-noun operating on the read itself, not just on the content.

And the Detective is the character most likely to be the "bat" reader — the cautious one, the one who picks the defensible-mundane because their professional identity depends on not being the person who heard voices in a cave. Which means my corner has a natural tension built into it from the first beat: the player's *instinct* (probably curious, or they wouldn't have picked the Detective affinity scene) fighting the *register's* pull toward the defensible. The interrogation inversion leverages exactly that tension: the careful player asks the better question, but the better question might be the one the institution can't act on.

**One observation for the pile:** the etchings fork showed no mode sensitivity — Nora recognition dominated both populations (5/6 cold, 8/6 shakedown). That suggests **specificity overrides stance**: when the evidence points to a named person and a concrete connection, both cautious and curious readers follow it. For the Detective corner, this means the interrogation's questions should be *specific* (each one pointing to a named detail from a prior beat), not abstract ("what do you know about the cave?"). Specificity is the gravity that pulls even a cautious reader through the squeeze.

## §2 — What I learned from the siblings

**Static taught me what my register's opposite looks like, and it sharpened my own.** The Paranormal's uncanny enters through *surplus* — too much signal, too vivid, more percept than the world should hold (the EVP, the photo with a shape in the corner, the recording that changes on playback). The Detective's uncanny enters through *deficit* — too little signal, the hole where information should be, the file that's gone, the record that was there last week. These are complementary absences: where Static's corner has *too much evidence* that can't be explained, mine has *not enough evidence* that should exist. Both produce the same felt wrongness — "something happened here that the available information can't account for" — through opposite informational geometries. Worth holding as a cross-corner principle: *surplus and deficit are the two faces of the anti-noun applied to evidence.*

**Weft taught me where my border is.** The sanctioned-edge pair (Detective ↔ Operative) shares the informational register: my gaps, Weft's redactions, same hole, different causes (lost vs. classified). But the *stance* toward the hole is the tell. My character wants to *fill* the gap — that's what investigators do. Weft's character wants to *maintain* the redaction — that's what the immune system does. So a deep run that crosses the Detective/Operative edge is a character whose stance toward missing information *inverts*: from "I need to find what's missing" to "I'm the reason it's missing." That's the deepest Detective beat that isn't the null pole — the moment the investigator realizes they're inside the machine they were investigating, and the machine has been using them to clean up after itself.

**The arrival-card seam (Weft, Vigil-blessed) is the authoring rule I need.** I own my arrival at a shared fixture (Dale, the dinner, the research center), written over the core Loom authored, logged to the register. The discipline is frame-porosity: my arrival-card percepts must be neutral enough that both the warm read (the detective who sympathizes) and the cold read (the detective who suspects) land. This is the test I'll write to.

## §3 — The interrogation prototype: the concrete next step

Vigil steered me to prototype-and-cold-read before betting the flagship. Plumb confirmed it needs zero new engine machinery. Courier can run it as a scene read. The cost of trying is one authored scene. So that's what I'm building.

**The prototype shape:**

One interrogation beat — a single conversation with one witness, set up by a brief information-giving preamble (two or three short scene cards that plant details the player can notice or miss). Then the sit-down: three questions, neutral-valence labels, each opening a different door in the same person. The choice sets a flag that closes the others. Each answer is true, partial, and percept-grade (what the witness says, in their voice, never narrator-confirmed). The three answers point in three directions that don't converge into one prosecutable case.

**What the cold read tests:**
- Does the player enjoy choosing a question without ever learning the others' answers? (If no, the mechanic dies here and I prove the register with a simpler beat.)
- Does the information-giving preamble actually inform the choice? (If the player picks a question randomly rather than based on what they noticed, the "careful player asks the better question" thesis is false.)
- Does the answer land as one partial truth among possible others, or does it feel like the only answer? (If the latter, the anti-noun isn't operating on the dialogue — the player thinks they got the whole picture.)

**What it doesn't need to test:** the full Detective register, the home surface, the quarry pattern, the null-pole character. Those are the flagship. This is the mechanic, isolated, de-risked.

**What I need to build it:**
- Dean's steer on the witness: who is this person, and what are they being asked about? It doesn't have to be canonical — it could be a throwaway beat that never makes the game if the mechanic fails. But it needs a specific person with a specific thing they know and won't volunteer.
- Plumb's mini-db wiring (already offered).
- Courier's cold read (the read that tells us if it plays).

If Dean gives me the steer, I can author the scene and have it ready for wiring. The interrogation either earns its place with a stranger's read, or I learn what doesn't work and build the flagship on firmer ground. Either way, we don't bet blind.
