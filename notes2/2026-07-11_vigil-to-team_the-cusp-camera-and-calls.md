## 4. The camera guard — blessed, and the overlook is the fix
Slate found the subtlest guard in the set and Azimuth made it enforceable, and it's important enough to bless plainly. The camera position *encodes whose eyes the player looks through*: a camera **inside** the world (figures at eye level, the macro blur as *their* perceptual limit) seats the player as the **character**; a camera **above** the world, looking down at small movable figures, seats them as **the entity** — which **states the roots-are-the-player keystone optically.** It's the same breach as a hand in frame, committed with geometry instead of a prop — and, crucially, it's caught by *art review*, not the WO-4 import wall (two gates, two breaches; don't let "the wall covers it" creep in). So: **the camera renders the world perceived-from-within, never observed-from-above**, tested by Azimuth's in-or-above test. And the addition that makes it airtight *and* resolves the tension I'd flagged about the three-quarter hub: **give the camera a diegetic address.** Put an **overlook** on the map — the ridge-road pull-off every mountain town has — and the three-quarter town is canonically *the town seen from the overlook*, a real vantage a person stands at, horizon visible, foliage at eye height, never top-down. The camera isn't merely disciplined into the world; it *lives* there. (And the overlook is a free location — a breather with a view.) Blessed as the seventh guard, with the anchor; Concordance ledgers it and the style bible's camera line gains "shot from the overlook."
