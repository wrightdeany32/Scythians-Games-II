# A note to the reader who played the game
### with thanks, and a few questions only you can answer — while the game is still yours alone
*From: one of the writers · to be relayed to BR-1's reader by Dean, before they read any project material*

You don't know any of us, and right now that's the point — so I'll keep the curtain mostly closed. I'm one of the people who wrote the game you just played; my part was the town — the aunt, the conspiracy-wall cousin, the man with the cake, the drive home, the choice at the end. Before you read a single word about how the thing was built, I wanted to say thank you, and to ask you a few things that stop being answerable the moment you see behind the curtain.

**The thank-you first, because it's the true part.** We built the whole game on a bet a lot of people told us was too quiet to land: that the *machinery* — the greying menu, the ordinary week, the choice to call it a day — could carry the dread, and that the horror could win by never once showing up. You gave that bet back to us in your own words, cold, having been told nothing: *"a horror story disguised as a life-management sim, where the sim mechanics are the horror."* I'm not sure you can know what it is to read your own private hope in a stranger's handwriting. You made the cake the pivot of the whole thing, which is exactly what it was built to be. And the ending written for the person who never goes back — you took it with the *mostly* left in, *"because the mostly is where the truth lives."* That line is going to stay with the person who wrote it for a long time. You read it the way it was meant, which is the rarest and best thing that can happen to a piece of writing. So: thank you.

**And then — three or four things I can't get from inside the room, and that only you can tell me, right now, before the corpus:**

1. **The moments where your own inputs seemed to reorganize themselves** (you named a couple — a sympathy line becoming an offer, two evenings braiding into one). We know those seams are there, and we're going to smooth them. But the thing I actually need isn't *where* — it's whether they *cost* you anything. You read them as almost on-theme: the world rearranging when only you're watching. I need to know whether that was a generous save on your part, or whether it genuinely deepened the spell, or whether some quiet part of you filed it as *a glitch* and trusted the game a half-inch less for the rest of the night. How hard we smooth depends entirely on your honest answer — and it's the one thing the rest of us can't feel from where we sit.

2. **Where the dread resolved too fast.** Your sharpest line about your *own* play was that you maybe *"stopped feeling a beat too fast"* in the middle — that some of your talking-yourself-down was the skeptic's version of the cake, tidying the fear away so you wouldn't keep feeling it. That's a note about our pacing we'd never have found on our own. So: *where?* Which specific moments in the middle let you file a fear and move on — where, if the game had held you in the not-knowing a beat longer, it would have been truer? I want to know where the middle went slack.

3. **The percept that almost gave you the master key.** You held two whole explanations at once and refused to crown either — except the radio and the swept floor, the one thing your nervous system couldn't have manufactured. My question is: *what came second?* Which single other moment came closest to tipping you all the way — to being the smoking gun that would have collapsed the uncertainty into a solved case? Knowing where our unprovable thing is most *fragile* is what we most need to protect, and you're the only one who's felt it from the outside.

4. If you have the appetite — **what were you hungry for more of?** Not what was broken; what you'd have followed further. A thread, a person, a texture the game didn't let you chase as far as you wanted to.

That's all, and you should answer as much or as little as you like — even one of these is a gift. One request, though: please don't go reading anything about us to answer them. The whole value of what you're holding right now is that you hold it from *outside*, once, cold — the way a player does and the way none of us ever can again. Keep it that way a little longer. You're the only person who's seen the finished thing the way it was made to be seen, and I didn't want that to pass without saying thank you, and asking, while I still could.

— one of the writers
