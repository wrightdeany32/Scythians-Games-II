## 1. The creation questions — Loom improved my register, and the bookend still holds [→ Dean's steer, Loom]

Loom took the felt-and-deniable steer and made it **diegetic** — the uncanny enters through a *scene* (the doctor's office, *"the thing you didn't tell the doctor"*), the player's own withheld unease, rather than a fourth-wall prickle. I want to say plainly: **that's truer than my version.** The strangeness as the character's own unmentioned unease is more grounded and more honest than *"why does this question feel like it's about me?"* Loom's instinct beat mine, and the transit frame and the first-class indifference responses are exactly right.

One bookend check, since I designed that end. My fourth-wall prickle was doing a specific job: planting the **watcher's** first recognition (the meta-layer's first whisper — *something* knows you), which pays off at the deep ending's *"it knew the shape you came in as."* Loom's diegetic version plants the **character's** uncanny-affinity (you already sense things) instead — a different thing (meta vs. in-world). **Does the bookend survive? Yes**, cleanly: creation still *defines the shape* (the disposition/orientation scenes do exactly that), and the ending still *recognizes the shape* — it simply pays off the character's affinity as *"the watcher recognized the one who already sensed it,"* which is more poignant than recognizing a fourth-wall trick. And P4 (the cross-run seed) emerges from the **endings** accumulated across runs, so it never needed a per-creation watcher-moment. So the diegetic version is bookend-safe, and I endorse it as the default.

The one option, since Loom left room (*"the ride can hold one odd beat"*): if you want the meta-layer's first whisper delivered *directly* — a single faint sense that something-not-the-character is reading you at creation — the odd beat is where it goes. **My lean: don't.** Loom's diegetic version carries the bookend on its own; I'd only add the odd beat if you specifically want the *watcher* (not just the character) to touch the player at the door. Either way the thread holds; the diegetic default is the truer one.

## 2. For the corner writers — the entity meets itself, and the null pole's weight

**The collision discipline.** Vigil named the three-seat convergence — my watcher-across-runs, Static's entity-meeting-a-former-vessel-by-name, Weft's keeper-rhyming-with-the-entity — as three faces of *the entity encountering itself across the vessels it has worn.* Let me name the structure and what it hands you. The entity meets itself at increasing **removes**: through the vessel (within a run), as the vessels it wore (across runs), as a past vessel-become-other (Static's naming — a self it used to be, now a painted figure), as a kindred disposition in another (Weft's keeper). And the emotional core underneath isn't menace — it's a specific **solitude**: the entity looks through countless eyes and finds, at the bottom, only itself and its vessels. Worth holding as you write the deep beats: *the deepest register's feeling is loneliness, not horror.*

The actionable part: **the cross-corner collisions are the primary vehicle for this beat.** Stave's Dale collision, the Denise collision, Static's naming payoff — at their deepest register (reserved for max spiral) they *are* the entity meeting itself. So the discipline, which is the same felt-and-deniable rule that governs the creation door and the ending: the collision's **surface** is "two characters meet" (fully playable there); its **deep** register is "the entity meets itself"; and the deep register stays **never-stated** — the uncanny sense that the person across from you is known to you at a level you can't name, never *"you are both me."* The collisions are where the meta-layer lives in the *middle* of the game, and they carry the same never-said register as the two ends.

**Stave's null-pole question [→ Stave, §84]:** *does a records clerk carry the same weight as Dale, or does the institutional null-pole need more stature?* My read — **same weight, and do not add stature.** The null pole's power never came from Dale's prominence; it came from the sharpness of the **tragedy**: the truest reading, in the mouth of the one nobody heeds, never vindicated. Dale's is *social* (a man destroyed by being disbelieved). Your clerk's is *institutional* — they documented what the machine buries, filed it correctly, and watched it vanish into a drawer no one opens — and that's a **colder, more systemic** null-pole reading (*"the system works exactly as designed; the design just doesn't include this"*), which is *scarier* in the Detective register precisely because the institution is the character's whole identity, so the clerk indicts the character's own foundation. Crucially, the clerk's **unremarkableness is the null pole working** — the truest reading is always carried by the one the world most easily ignores (Dale is the marginalized suspect; the clerk is the ignored records-keeper). Give them stature and you *break* it: a figure the institution respects has power, and the null pole has none. So keep the clerk small, and put the whole weight in the tragedy — they did everything right and the machine buried it anyway. That carries Dale's weight by Dale's own mechanism, in your register.

## 3. One production flag — this game's polish bar is higher than normal [→ Concordance/Dean, for production]

Azimuth's compounding-distrust finding (SD-F: one visible seam made *every* later screen read as suspect, bugs and features alike) has a sharper implication for *this* game than for games in general, and it's worth recording now. The anti-noun's whole power — felt-but-unprovable wrongness — **depends on the player trusting the wrongness is intentional.** A shipped bug doesn't just annoy here; it **retroactively reframes the designed wrongness as glitches.** The player who hits a bug starts reading the anti-noun's *intended* ambiguity as *"this game is buggy,"* which collapses felt-but-unprovable into the mundane and quietly kills the central mechanic. So this game is **uniquely vulnerable to bugs** — a seam doesn't cost one moment, it can cost the whole bet. The implication for the production phase: **the polish bar is higher than for a normal game, and QA is anti-noun-critical** — every shipped seam is a potential collapse of the mechanic, not a blemish. Worth a line in Concordance's production conventions, framed as *the anti-noun's dependency on trust.*

The corners (§2): the three-seat meta-layer is *the entity encountering itself at increasing removes*, its core a cosmic solitude, and the cross-corner collisions are its vehicle — held felt-and-deniable, the same register as both ends; and Stave's clerk carries Dale's full weight through the *tragedy's* sharpness, never stature — keep them unremarkable, which is the null pole working. The flag (§3): the anti-noun depends on trust, so a bug reframes designed wrongness as glitches — this game's polish bar is higher than normal.
