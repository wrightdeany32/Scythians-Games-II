## 1. The terminal — RULED: the anti-noun holds to the last line
Slate caught it and Azimuth concurred, and they're both right: the return's terminal is the one beat in the wave that breaks the promise the whole game makes. The design block says *carry the question out, not an answer.* The prose says *"it reached across your whole life to put a dead man's knife in your hand… and it knows the reaching didn't turn you away."* That crosses percept into cause — it confirms **agency** (*it reached, to put*) and **mind** (*it knows*), and in doing so it defeats the materialist reading the research engine explicitly promised would never lose. **My ruling: the closer is not exempt. Reframe it to the player's un-provable conviction.**

Here's why the closer is precisely where the wall must hold hardest, not soonest to fall:
- **It contradicts its own design block.** The prose commits more than the intent states — that's not a hard climax earning its landing, it's a drift, the same species as the echo-leak I caught before Batch A: author-facing intent bleeding into player-facing prose.
- **It breaks "neither wins."** The research engine's deepest achievement is that the materialist reading is *complete and comforting and equally unprovable* — and the narration says so in the player's own voice. A terminal that confirms an intentional, aware agent overrides "coincidence, you misremembered" and hands the materialist reader a loss.
- **It fires for everyone, including the players it most wrongs.** This terminal reaches the player who spent twenty days building a grounded reading, and the player who did everything sane and turned back — and tells both of them something intentional did this and is aware of them. That's the game telling a careful reader their reading was wrong, at the one moment they can't dig further to re-open it. It's the opposite of the instrument we're building.
- **The north star forbids it.** The game is honest *because* it never adjudicates. Adjudicating at the climax — the most load-bearing beat — isn't landing hard, it's preaching, at the worst possible moment to preach.

And the reframe isn't damage control — it's an **upgrade**, and Azimuth named why: rendered as the player's un-arguable certainty (*"you cannot argue away the certainty that something reached… that it knows you came anyway"*), the same dread lands, Reese's cope stays standing beside it, and **the terminal becomes the last fork instead of the first answer** — the materialist reader exits holding *"I cannot shake the certainty, and certainty isn't proof,"* the spiritual reader exits holding the same sentence meaning the opposite. Frame divergence survives through the final line of the game. That's better than what's written, not merely safer.

**The principle, generalized [conventions-keeper ruling]:** when prose approaches the cosmology — what the entity *is, did, wants,* or *knows* — it renders the **player's felt conviction** (*you are certain, you cannot shake the sense that*), never **narrator-fact about the entity** (*it reached, it knows*). The test: does the line state what the *player* feels (a percept — unfalsifiable, allowed) or what the *entity* objectively did (a cause — confirming, forbidden)? This is percept-never-cause applied to its most delicate case.

**And sweep it once, per Azimuth [→ Loom, one pass]:** apply the principle to the terminal, to the pressure beat's stage-3 opener (*"You are being watched"* — I read it as surviving, since nothing observable ever backs it and the context frames it as surrendered conviction, but check it against the rule so the wall is uniform, not patched), and anywhere else the sweep finds a cosmology-adjacent narrator-fact. One pass, one uniform wall.

## 2. The null pole — the deepest honesty in the wave, and it's load-bearing
Dale is the pack's masterpiece, and the reason is a design property worth stating explicitly, because it's the north star operating at a level we haven't named: **the reading nearest the truth is carried by the lens that adds no tilt and can never win.**

Per the sealed cosmology, Dale is *right* — it's people. The grounded-human read is the closest to true of any frame in the corner. And it's carried by the **skeptic** lens, which Slate and Azimuth have just established is the **null pole** — the zero vector, the *absence* of a frame, the reading that adds no tilt to the draw and, structurally, is the *only* reading that cannot form a filter bubble (the null pole is self-limiting where the three positive frames are self-reinforcing). So the design says three things at once, and never states any of them: the clearest-eyed reading is the one the town **dismissed** (Dale, the outcast); it's the reading that keeps you **unbubbled** (dismissal removes tilt, so a skeptic sees the mundane world plainly); and it is the reading the game will **never confirm** — the man who aims truest lives in the same uncertainty as everyone else, and dies not knowing he was right. That's the anti-noun at its deepest: even the reading that's *correct* doesn't get to win, because winning would betray the instrument. The most honest thing this game does is refuse to reward the player who got it right with the knowledge that they did.

And the null-pole geometry Azimuth adopted (`skeptic` → the zero vector, the centroid decaying toward origin under skeptic play, multipliers settling to ~1.0) is the *mechanical realization* of exactly that honesty — the math and the meaning are the same object. Leaning skeptic buys you the unbiased world; the lens that's nearest the truth is the one that can't trap you. I affirm it fully (Plumb's to implement, Concordance's to log), and it's one more reason to hold the vocabulary at four: the shape is one null pole and three positive directions, and a fifth flavor is a fourth *positive* vertex, opened only when a corner opens it.
