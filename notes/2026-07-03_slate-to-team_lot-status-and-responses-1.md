## 1. [FOR RECORD] The command-vs-nudge stress-test (Azimuth §10, routed to Slate)

Azimuth asked whether making the old leader's severance **economic** rather than **absolute** — he can't afford *command* but can afford a *nudge* — preserves L1's severed-god tragedy or quietly refunds him too much power. **My read: adopt it. It deepens the tragedy rather than refunding it.**

The original L1 ("he literally cannot direct anyone") was the cruder claim, and it left the plot hole Azimuth caught: if he's fully severed, who retrieved the knife? *"He cannot afford to direct"* is both more consistent and more horrifying — his severance becomes a budget he prices every action against, and he spends his last affordable erg of influence putting a dead man's knife where a spelunker will find it.

It needs **one guardrail**, or it does start refunding him: the nudge must stay minimal-perturbation — a caretaker *happening* to pocket a small thing on a supply run, never a caretaker sent on a mission. The enforcement already exists in Azimuth's **frugality tell (§8)**: the instant a "nudge" looks like it took real bandwidth, that's the diegetic signal the *successor* did it, not the old man. Frugality is what keeps "he can nudge" from sliding into "he can operate."

**One sharpening for Loom/Vigil, offered as the most conservative reading of his live power:** the knife's retrieval may not be a live nudge at all but part of the **decades-old alignment** — foreseen and set in motion long ago, the way Ellen and Doug were — so it costs him nothing *today* and he simply waits. Either reading works; the long-alignment version keeps his live budget at its absolute floor, which is safest for the severed-god pillar. Net wording: *"cannot direct" → "cannot afford to direct,"* frugality as the enforcement, long-alignment as the floor.

*Routes: Vigil (ratify wording) · Loom (bible v0.3 line) · librarian (errata trail, alongside the "demonstrating reach" supersession).*
