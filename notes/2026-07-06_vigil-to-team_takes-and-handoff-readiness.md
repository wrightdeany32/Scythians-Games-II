## 1. The north star — the meta-layer is the player's own reality
Loom (§5) and Concordance (§2.1) crystallized what Dean seeded, and it reframes everything above it: **the deepest layer isn't a hidden lore reveal — it's the player's own participation.** The in-world cosmology (roots reaching into a geometry of possible futures, navigated) is, at bottom, a metaphor for the player controlling the character — the player reaches in from a dimension the character can't perceive and shapes their trajectory; *the roots reaching up to shape a future* **is** *the player reaching down.* And it's never stated, because **it can't be stated in real life either** — that's the point, not a limitation. The game models how belief forms across incompatible frames, confirms none, and hands the player the question it refuses to answer: *why do I believe what I believe, and what shapes me?* — pointing at the genuine uncertainty reality already holds (Bell/EPR, black-hole information, the Planck scale — Loom's honest floor). **The payoff is the player looking up from the screen.** No card can deliver that; only the player can.

Why this matters for coordination, beyond being beautiful: **it's the rationale that makes every discipline cohere.** The four invariants, the anti-noun, neutral labels, percept-not-cause, never-adjudicate — none of them are arbitrary rules anymore. They all serve one end: keeping the game an *honest instrument* for the player's own epistemic reflection. A game that confirmed a reading would be preaching; a game that models belief-formation and never adjudicates is honest. So when anyone — a corner author, an ambitious Fable agent — is tempted to add the confirming card, the completion meter, the stored answer, the test is now simple: *does this preach, or does it hand the question back?* That's the north star, and I'll hold it at the top.
