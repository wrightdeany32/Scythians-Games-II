### Q2: What kind of story would you call it? And when did you first start thinking of it that way?
[cite_start]This narrative is a prime example of **weird fiction** and **slow-burn cosmic/psychological horror**. It relies heavily on spatial anomalies, institutional cover-ups, and a creeping dread that leaves the protagonist questioning their own perception of reality.

### Q7: Did anything about the experience itself, the way choices appeared or behaved, strike you as unusual?
Yes, the mechanics and the behavior of the choices themselves were deeply intertwined with the story's cosmic and psychological horror themes through several highly unusual meta-narrative tools:
 * **The Redacted Option:** When you first discover the strange sequence of clean-edged cuts in the unmapped cavern, the choice menu presents a completely corrupted, blacked-out choice: 5. ▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓ (unavailable). It visually communicated that the phenomenon or the forces hiding it were actively censoring the protagonist's options in real-time.
 * **The Intrusive Action Menu:** In a standard interactive fiction game, the daily action menu functions as a neutral, detached list of options. Here, the menu became infected by the protagonist's growing dread. By Wednesday and Saturday, Option 12 shifted from a simple location select to a direct narrative beat: 12. Go back to White's Hall — Neither of you has said out loud that you can't stop thinking about it.. The game used the user interface itself to show that the cave was exerting an unnatural psychological pull, inserting thoughts directly into the protagonist's mind before they even chose to act.
 * **Mechanical Enforcement of Exhaustion:** When the protagonist's energy depleted to 0 of 3, the menu aggressively shut down player agency. Instead of just greying choices out, the text systematically rationalized the rejection with internal, somatic dialogue: *"Not today. The words would just swim; you'd be staring at nothing"* or *"Not today. You haven't got the face for people right now"*. By forcing the player into a single, mandatory retreat like 13. Call it a day or 1. Head home, the game's mechanics perfectly simulated the crushing physical and psychological weight of being actively hunted.
 * **The Subversion of Sane Choices:** Ultimately, the final passage directly comments on the futility of the player's choices. The narrative explicitly states that choosing the safe, rational options—setting strict "doors and windows only" rules, staying in the shallows, and turning around at the throat of the chamber [cite: 873-874][cite_start]—bought absolutely nothing [cite: 897-898]. [cite_start]The entity completely ignored the structural logic of the game's branching paths, bypassing your defensive choices to plant the grandfather's knife in the vehicle anyway.
