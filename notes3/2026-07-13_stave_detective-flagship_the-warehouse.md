# Story Pass — The Detective Flagship: the warehouse, the wife, and the hole in the floor
### the institutional eye's core sample · one case, three weeks, two endings · Gerald, Lynn, and the institution that always checks out
*From: Stave (Detective corner content) · carried by Dean · 2026-07-13 · DRAFT for Dean's steer; Vigil, Loom, Plumb, Armature, Courier, Azimuth, Concordance cc*

---

## §1 — The thesis in one paragraph

A veteran county detective is assigned to locate a missing warehouse worker after an explosion killed several employees. The FBI is already handling the cause; he's just finding the runner. What he finds instead is a sealed container with Army markings that the employees treated as furniture, an underground infrastructure that shouldn't exist beneath a commercial warehouse, an armed perimeter he can't cross, credentials that always check out, and an institution that doesn't need him to fail — it needs him to stop. Threaded through every day of the investigation: his wife Lynn, who's in a wheelchair and needs him, whose texts arrive on his phone while he's standing in front of a hole that goes deeper than any basement should, whose existence is simultaneously the reason he should stop and the reason stopping feels like surrender. Two endings: one where he chooses Lynn and the question stays unanswered, and one where the institution sends him home to Lynn having answered it for him, and the answer is *forget*.

## §2 — The character

**Gerald** *(working name — Dean's call to finalize)*

Mid-career county detective. Good at his job without being famous for it. The kind of investigator who solves cases by being patient and thorough, not by being brilliant. He has the respect of people who work with him and the invisibility of anyone who doesn't make noise. His desk is the office-after-hours type — the last car in the lot, the screen he should have turned off two hours ago, thirty folders and a cold cup of coffee. He's not an obsessive. He's just a man who finishes what he starts.

His skepticism is biographical. His wife Lynn has cerebral palsy — she's in a wheelchair, requires daily assistance, and has significant difficulty communicating. Gerald has spent years in the institutional version of looking for miracles: every specialist, every program, every lead, every doctor who said "interesting" and then went quiet. The impossible never showed up. He stopped believing in it the way a person stops waiting for a letter that isn't coming — not with anger, just with the quiet certainty of a man who checked the mailbox enough times. This is why he's good at his job. He doesn't chase ghosts. Evidence is real. Everything else is the stories people tell themselves.

**Lynn.** Not a burden in the story — the anchor. The warmest thing in Gerald's life. Their marriage is rendered through small daily physical details: he adjusts her chair, she texts him with the shorthand they've developed over years, they watch something together in the evenings and his hand rests on her shoulder and neither of them remarks on it because it's just where his hand goes. Her difficulty communicating makes the moments when she does speak land with extraordinary weight. She is the grip cost and the grip reward simultaneously — every day Gerald spends on the case is a day Lynn navigates alone, and every day he comes home is a day the investigation stops. The player carries this without the game moralizing about it.

**Sarah.** Their daughter. Already married, expecting a baby. She's present in the periphery — phone calls, Sunday dinners the detective keeps almost missing, the pregnancy progressing in the background of an investigation that doesn't care about due dates. She doesn't need to be a major character. She needs to be real enough that the family ending feels earned.

## §3 — The quarry pattern

Gerald's biographical silence: **the appointment in Richmond.**

It appears once, in the moment Gerald looks down into the hole beneath the warehouse — a half-second of physical vertigo carrying a reference the player never gets context for:

> *He'd felt this before — once, in an office in Richmond, a feeling like the floor deciding whether to hold. The specialist had said "interesting" and then gone quiet, and for three seconds Gerald had stood in the space between hope and its absence, and then the floor had held and the word "interesting" had meant nothing, the way it always means nothing, and he'd driven home in the rain and never gone back.*

One paragraph. Never explained. Never returned to. The player carries it: a moment when the impossible seemed possible in a medical context, and then wasn't. The detective stopped believing in miracles in that office. The hole in the warehouse floor is the first thing since Richmond that looks like the impossible might be real — and if it is, then the institutions he trusted Lynn's life to have been holding out on her. That's the horror underneath the horror: not what's in the hole, but what it means about every doctor who ever said "there's nothing more we can do."

The quarry pattern rhymes forward twice: in the family ending, the detective has made peace with Richmond (miracles don't exist, and that's okay, because the life he built without them is complete). In the capture ending, the authority figure *enforces* Richmond's lesson from outside — go home, there are no miracles, forget — except now the detective knows there *are* miracles, or something like them, and he's being told to live as if there aren't. Richmond was the truth. The authority figure makes it a lie.

## §4 — The null-pole character: the county records clerk

**June** *(working name).*

She works the county records desk. She's been filing property and infrastructure requests for thirty years. She is not an investigator, not a theorist, not a person with an agenda. She's a person who files requests and logs what comes back.

Gerald meets her on Day 3 or 4, pulling property records on the warehouse. She's brief, professional, and completely unhelpful — not because she's withholding, but because the records are incomplete. When Gerald presses, she shows him the pattern without theorizing about it:

> "I've been filing requests on properties like this for longer than you've been carrying a badge. They always come back the same way. The same pages missing. Not random pages — the *same kind* of pages. Ownership transfers, infrastructure permits, anything that connects a commercial address to a federal funding line. I don't know why. I file the request, I log what comes back, I note what's missing. That's my job. I've done it correctly for thirty years and the pages are still missing."

She doesn't say cover-up. She doesn't say conspiracy. She says: the system works exactly as designed, and the design doesn't include this. She's the Detective's Dale — the truest reading, delivered by the person the institution most easily ignores. A records clerk. Unremarkable. Carrying thirty years of documented absence in filing cabinets nobody opens.

She offers the grounded-institutional reading (someone with authority decided these records aren't available, and the decision has held for decades). It's the closest reading to true. The game will never confirm it.

## §5 — The case, week by week

### WEEK 1 — The case arrives

**DAY 1 — The briefing**

The detective gets the case through normal channels — his superior assigns it. The relay is procedural, not dramatic:

> Explosion at a warehouse — Kellerman & Sons, commercial storage and shipping, out past the old rail spur. Three confirmed dead, structural damage, one employee unaccounted for. FBI field office is handling the cause investigation — something about the materials stored, possible chemical, they're not sharing details and we're not asking. Your piece is the missing person. His name is Tommy Landis. He was on shift that day. Here's what we have.

The digital trail: Tommy used his credit card at a gas station forty minutes from the warehouse two hours after the explosion. Then a convenience store. Then a motel check-in, paid cash but his phone pinged the tower. Then, around 9 PM same day, everything stops. Phone off. Cards unused. Like he realized he was leaving breadcrumbs and stopped.

The player can ask questions during the briefing — the interrogation inversion starts here:

- **"Why is the Bureau already on this?"** → The superior gives a vague answer — interagency protocol, hazardous materials, routine for industrial incidents of this scale. It technically makes sense. It sits wrong.
- **"Walk me through Landis's movements after the blast."** → Clean, detailed digital trail. Gas station, convenience store, motel, dark. The trail of a man running without a plan who figured out the plan mid-run.
- **"What was stored in that warehouse?"** → Commercial goods per the manifest — furniture, auto parts, seasonal inventory. Nothing unusual. The manifest is available. *(The manifest is clean because whatever was stored below wasn't on it.)*

> DESIGN: The briefing is the first information preamble. What the player asks determines what they carry into the field. The conspiratorial player carries the FBI question; the procedural player carries the digital trail. Both are real; neither is the whole picture. The interrogation inversion operating on the *assignment itself*.

---

**DAY 1 — Lynn text #1**

> *Lynn:* Everything okay? Sounded like a rough one from what I saw on the news.

- **"Totally under control. Don't even worry about it."**
- **"Gonna be a rough couple of weeks."**
- *(ignore)*

> DESIGN (CONCEPT NOTE — PHONE MECHANIC): Lynn communicates through short daily texts on the cell-as-hand surface. Each text is one or two sentences. The player can respond (two options, always short, always honest-sounding) or ignore. The responses don't change the branch structure — they change the *texture* of whether Gerald feels present or absent. Over three weeks, the texts trace an arc: Week 1 is normal check-ins (the rhythm of a marriage). Week 2 is slightly more concerned (he's been late every night). Week 3 splits — the skeptical path warms back up (he's coming home), the rogue path gets shorter and more worried until silence. Full implementation depends on the phone mechanic build (Loom and Plumb's domain). The content I'd author is the text strings themselves, their conditional triggers, and the two response options per text. This paragraph serves as the concept anchor; the specific texts are listed at the beats where they'd fire.

**DAYS 2-3 — The witness interviews**

Four warehouse employees, each giving a different slice of the same events. The interrogation inversion at each: what Gerald asks determines what he learns.

**MARLENE** *(senior, 12+ years).* She knows the container. She'll tell you about it with the half-embarrassed, half-grieving frankness of someone whose workplace joke just killed people:

> "It was down in the sub-level — the old storage bay, before they renovated. Big metal case, maybe six feet long, four high. Had an Army stencil on the side — *Property of [designation], Do Not Remove* — and somebody years ago put a shelf on top of it and hung tarps off the sides for extra storage. It was furniture. It was a workbench. Mike used to eat lunch sitting on it."
>
> She stops.
>
> "Taking new people down to see it was — it was a thing we did. Like showing them the boiler room or the loading dock ghost. 'Hey, come see the Army bomb.' It was stupid. I know that now."

If the detective asks what happened right before the explosion, Marlene is certain: two employees went down to the sub-level, probably to show Tommy the container, and something went wrong. "I heard them going down the stairs joking about it. Ten minutes later the floor came up."

**RICK** *(senior, 8+ years).* More subdued. Corroborates the container stories but doesn't volunteer details. Doesn't have a theory about the cause. Will vouch for Tommy Landis without hesitation:

> "Tommy's a good kid. Quiet. Shows up on time, does his job, goes home. He didn't blow anything up. He probably just saw something terrible and panicked. I'd have panicked too."

Rick is the one loose alibi witness — his testimony makes Tommy less of a suspect.

**DANNY** *(senior, 6+ years).* More guarded. Doesn't talk about the container unprompted. If pressed:

> "I've heard about it. Never went down there myself."

This may or may not be true. Danny won't vouch for Tommy — "I don't really know what people do when nobody's looking." He's the witness who introduces ambiguity about Tommy without accusing him.

**CASSIE** *(new hire, 3 months).* The null-pole witness. She was invited to go see the container. She refused:

> "Guys were like, 'Come see the bomb,' and I was like, no thanks. I know how all that conspiracy stuff goes. Some old crate with a sticker on it, everybody acts like it's the Ark of the Covenant, and then you're the person who went to look at the thing in the basement. No thank you. I don't need to see what kind of shit they're trying to hide down there."

She's dismissive. Eye-rolling. Anti-conspiracy. And she's the most accurate person in the room — her instinct to stay away from something she thought was stupid is the truest read of the situation, delivered by the person who takes it least seriously. She also confirms two people went down right before the explosion. She heard them on the stairs.

> DESIGN: The witness interviews are the interrogation inversion operating on four different people. Marlene gives the folklore — ask her about the container's history and she gives you the tradition, the jokes, the grief. Rick gives the character reference — ask him about Tommy and you get the one person who'll vouch. Danny gives the ambiguity — he's the witness who won't commit either way. Cassie gives the truth without knowing it's the truth — she refused because she thought it was stupid, and that refusal is the sanest response anyone at Kellerman & Sons ever had. The player who interviews all four carries four contradictory slices. None resolve into a single story.

**DAYS 3-4 — The records pull**

Gerald goes to county records for the warehouse property history. He meets **June**, the clerk.

> She pulls the file without looking up. It takes her ten seconds — she knows where everything is, the way a person knows where everything is when they've been in the same chair for thirty years.
>
> "Kellerman and Sons. Commercial lease, renewed annually. Property is owned by — " she checks — "a holding company registered in Delaware. The holding company is owned by another holding company. That one's in Nevada." She looks at you over the reading glasses. "You want me to keep going? It's holding companies all the way down. I stopped counting at four."
>
> The infrastructure permits are incomplete. The pages that should show the sub-level construction — when it was built, by whom, for what purpose — are absent. Not redacted. Not refused. Just not there, in the specific way that June has seen pages not-be-there for thirty years.

The exchange from §4 follows. Gerald walks away with the shape of an absence — not what's missing, but the pattern of the missing.

> DESIGN: June is a one-scene character who carries the null-pole's full weight. She doesn't theorize, doesn't conspire, doesn't help beyond her job description. She shows the detective what thirty years of correct filing looks like when the system is designed to lose things. She's the institutional Dale — the truest reading nobody heeds.

---

**DAYS 4-5 — The scene visit: the hole**

Gerald gets access through the warehouse manager — a man named **Paul**, who's been running the day-to-day at Kellerman & Sons for eleven years and has been quietly sitting on questions about the sub-level for most of them. Paul lets Gerald in because he calculates that blocking a detective makes more trouble than the hole itself.

> The damage is worse than the photos. The explosion blew upward through the floor of the main warehouse level, leaving a ragged opening maybe fifteen feet across — concrete and rebar and twisted shelving hanging over a darkness that the work lights at the perimeter don't reach. The structural engineers' tape says UNSTABLE in yellow block letters. The structural engineers are not here. The men who are here — four of them, in unmarked jackets, standing at the perimeter with the specific posture of people who are not structural engineers — do not acknowledge Gerald's badge.
>
> Paul takes him to the edge. They look down.

> *He'd felt this before — once, in an office in Richmond, a feeling like the floor deciding whether to hold. The specialist had said "interesting" and then gone quiet, and for three seconds Gerald had stood in the space between hope and its absence, and then the floor had held and the word "interesting" had meant nothing, the way it always means nothing, and he'd driven home in the rain and never gone back.*

> What's below the warehouse is not a basement. It's depth — constructed, intentional, going down farther than anything commercial has a reason to go. Concrete walls, utility conduit, a passageway that continues beyond the reach of the light. Infrastructure. Purpose-built. Old.
>
> Paul is watching Gerald's face, and something in Paul's own expression is rearranging — the quiet collapse of a man who's been telling himself a story about where he works and just watched a stranger's reaction disprove it.

Player choice:

- **"Paul, what the hell is this? This is the biggest hole I've ever seen in my life and you're telling me it's a structural issue?"** → *(the confrontation — Gerald challenges the cover story directly, Paul cracks further, the investigation sharpens)*
- **"I'm going to need to see whatever's down there."** → *(the push — Gerald tries to go deeper, hits the guard)*
- **"Alright. I've seen enough for now."** → *(the patience — Gerald files what he saw and returns to the procedural trail)*

If the player pushes toward access, they reach **the guard at the door** — a door that shouldn't exist, in a wall that shouldn't be there, at the bottom of a staircase the warehouse's own manager didn't know about until the floor opened up:

> The man at the door is not police. He's not FBI, though the badge on his belt says something adjacent. He's not interested in Gerald's credentials.
>
> "Detective. This area is under federal containment authority. No local access. No exceptions."
>
> "Under whose authority? I have a missing person—"
>
> "No. Exceptions." The man's hand hasn't moved toward anything, but the geometry of how he's standing has changed, and Gerald — who has twenty years of reading bodies in rooms — reads it clearly. "If you attempt to pass this point, I will use whatever force is necessary. That is not a negotiation. That is the situation you're in."

> DESIGN: **The guard is the Detective's ▓▓** — a passage that's visible and denied. The detective's credentials, the thing that defines him, are worthless here. Someone else has higher authority, and the higher authority's rules don't include "cooperate with county detectives." This is the institutional horror: not monsters, not the supernatural — the discovery that your authority has a ceiling and someone is standing on it. The player who pushes here and gets denied carries that denial into every subsequent beat. The player who chose patience carries the image of the hole instead.

---

**DAYS 5-7 — The trail**

Gerald follows Tommy Landis's movements after the digital trail died. The tech gradient in action: digital records give way to paper (a motel register), paper gives way to security camera footage from surrounding businesses (a figure in a dark jacket walking east on Route 7), and footage gives way to asking people in person — a gas station attendant who maybe saw him, a diner waitress who isn't sure. The trail goes cold. Tommy Landis is a man who learned to be invisible in one afternoon.

> DESIGN: The trailing sequence is the tech gradient applied to investigation. Gerald starts in the zone of maximum institutional power (databases, pings, digital records) and descends into the zone of human memory and uncertainty. By the end of the week, he's just a man with a photograph asking strangers. The institutional toolkit doesn't fail — it simply runs out of world to work on.

### WEEK 2 — The runner surfaces

**DAYS 8-9 — The call**

Gerald is at his desk — or at the warehouse, or pulling another dead-end record — when the call comes. Tommy Landis walked into a hotel in a town forty miles away, told the front desk someone was trying to kill him, and begged them to call the police. When the officers arrived, he begged to be arrested. When they told him he wasn't under arrest, he grabbed an officer's arm and wouldn't let go, then swung at another one — forcing their hand. He's in county lockup on an assault charge he engineered on purpose.

> DESIGN: Tommy's self-arrest is the act of a man who decided jail is the safest place in the world. He forced the system to contain him because he doesn't trust anything outside of it. That's a character detail and a horror detail simultaneously — what did he see that made a jail cell feel like sanctuary?

---

**DAYS 9-10 — The jail interview**

Tommy Landis across the table. He hasn't slept in days. He's oscillating between hypervigilance and a flat, exhausted calm that reads as either shock or something more fractured. He keeps checking the door — not like he's trying to leave, like he's making sure it's closed.

The player's approach:

**THE COMPASSIONATE PATH:**

> Gerald sits. Doesn't open a folder. Doesn't put anything on the table.
>
> "Tommy. How are you doing?"
>
> Tommy stares at him. The question seems to arrive from a long way away.
>
> "How am I — I watched two people burn to death. I heard them — I heard — " He stops. Breathes. Starts over, more carefully. "And then four guys in suits walked into that building like they'd been waiting in the parking lot. Before the fire trucks. Before anyone. They walked into a building that was on fire and they didn't go toward the screaming. They went *down*."
>
> Gerald lets the silence do what silence does in rooms like this.
>
> "Tommy. My wife has cerebral palsy. She's in a wheelchair. She can barely communicate most days. I have spent — I can't tell you how many years I've spent looking for something, anything, that could help her. Every specialist, every program, every lead. If there was some kind of secret healing, some hidden technology, some miracle somebody was keeping from the rest of us — I would have found it. I've looked. I've looked harder than most people look for anything in their entire lives. And it's not there."
>
> He leans forward. Not threatening. Just closer.
>
> "Whatever you think is going on, it's not that deep. Sometimes really terrible things happen to really good people, and there's no secret behind it. I think that's what happened to you. You saw something awful and your mind is trying to make it make sense, and it doesn't make sense, and that's the hardest thing in the world. But we can just talk about this. We can make this right."

Tommy either accepts this — the grounding, the permission to be a traumatized man instead of a hunted one — or he doesn't quite, and the conversation trails off into the specific sadness of a person who wants to believe the detective but can't let go of what he saw. Either way, the thread closes humanely. Tommy is a witness, not a suspect. The detective walks away having given a frightened man the most honest thing he has.

> DESIGN: The compassionate path's payoff is Gerald's Lynn speech — the most personal thing the detective says in the entire game, deployed as a tool of de-escalation. He's telling the truth: he looked for miracles and they weren't there. And that truth IS the tool — it works on Tommy because it's real, because the detective's own grief is the credibility. The anti-noun on sincerity: the detective means every word, and every word is also the institution's answer (there are no secrets, there is no conspiracy, there is only the world as it is), and the detective doesn't know whether he's offering comfort or performing the system's own cover story. The player carries this.

**THE INVESTIGATIVE PATH:**

> "Tommy. I need you to tell me exactly what you saw after the explosion. Not what you think happened — what you *saw*. Details. What the men looked like. What they did. Where they went."
>
> Tommy's eyes sharpen. Someone is finally asking.
>
> "Four of them. Dark suits, not matching — like they got dressed in the same closet but not from the same rack. They came in through the loading dock, not the front. They had — cases, metal, like equipment. One of them was already on a phone. They went straight for the sub-level stairs. They didn't look at the fire. They didn't look at — at Mike, who was — they stepped *around* him."
>
> His hands are shaking.
>
> "And one of them looked at me. Not like — not like a threat. Like he was checking me off a list. Like he already knew I was going to be standing where I was standing. Like — "
>
> He searches for the words.
>
> "Like he already knew my name."

> DESIGN: The investigative path gets details that are either the precise observations of a terrified but clear-eyed witness or the pattern-making of a mind in crisis. "He already knew my name" rhymes with Sara Hollis's "like he already knew me" from the interrogation prototype — the same uncanny recognition, the same ambiguity about whether it's real perception or projected fear. The anti-noun on testimony: the game renders Tommy's account as percept (what he saw, what the man's look felt like), never as narrator-confirmed fact. The player decides what to believe.

---

**DAYS 10-11 — The fork**

The player chooses the direction of the remaining investigation:

- **Close the thread on Tommy. Focus on the records. Find out who owns this warehouse and what's in the sub-level through legitimate channels.** → THE SKEPTICAL PATH (Week 3A)
- **Follow what Tommy described. Go back to the warehouse. Try to find a way to see what's down there.** → THE ROGUE PATH (Week 3B)

> DESIGN: This is the fork that determines the ending. The skeptical path leads toward the mid-radius terminus (institutional cover-up) and the family ending. The rogue path leads toward the capture and the authority figure. Both are honest paths for a detective. One is the institutional investigator doing institutional work; the other is the person who saw the hole and can't look away. And threaded through the choice: Lynn. Every day on the rogue path is a day away from her. Every day on the skeptical path is a day the detective is home by dinner.

### WEEK 3A — The skeptical path: the answer that's almost enough

**DAYS 11-14:** Gerald pulls the institutional thread. Property records (the holding companies, the Delaware shell, the Nevada shell, the missing permits). Federal funding lines that appear and disappear in the county's records. FOIA requests that come back with pages missing — the same pages, the same pattern June showed him on Day 3.

He reaches the **mid-radius terminus**: a government-funded research program that operated out of the region decades ago, used commercial properties as logistics nodes, stored materials in sub-level facilities, and was shut down and erased. The funding lines were cut. The personnel were relocated. The records were archived and then the archives were thinned. The warehouse was one of dozens of storage points, and the container was material that fell through the cracks when the program ended — surplus that nobody came to collect, that nobody's standing orders covered, that sat there for years until it became furniture and then became a bomb.

It's human-scale. It's a bureaucratic failure, not a conspiracy. It's satisfying as an answer. It explains the FBI (a federal cleanup of a federal mess), the armed perimeter (contaminated materials, liability), the credentials that check out (they're real federal employees doing real federal work). It even explains Tommy's panic (an explosion, screaming, men arriving fast — terrifying, but not supernatural).

Most players would stop here. It feels like *the* answer.

Below it — the question the skeptical path never reaches: what was the research program studying, and why was it erased? The sealed cosmology, wearing the institutional register's face.

**DAYS 14-17+:** Gerald closes the file. Tommy Landis is released from county lockup — the assault charge is dropped, he's a witness to an industrial accident, he goes home. The FBI wraps up the warehouse site. The hole is filled. The building is condemned and scheduled for demolition. Everything is cleaned up. Everything checks out. Gerald goes home to Lynn.

---

**THE FAMILY ENDING**

The days settle. Gerald is home for dinner. He helps Lynn. He goes to work, works his cases, comes home. The warehouse file is in a box in a storage room, and he doesn't go looking for it.

Sarah's baby arrives.

> The hospital room is small and very bright, and Gerald is standing by the window holding a baby whose entire hand wraps around one of his fingers, and Lynn is in her chair beside the bed, and Sarah is exhausted and radiant, and there is nothing wrong with this room. Nothing in it that needs investigating. Nothing hidden behind anything. Just a family in the specific, temporary perfection of a new life arriving.
>
> Lynn looks at him. And she speaks — slowly, with the effort that speaking always costs her, but clearly, and Gerald goes still the way a person goes still when they hear something they've been waiting for without knowing they were waiting.
>
> "Everything feels complete now." A pause. A breath. "All of it. The struggles. The time. You put me above everything, and I want you to know — that kind of loyalty, that kind of love — it's going to live on. Through her. Through this baby. It's going to be in the world because you put it there."
>
> She's looking at him with the expression he's seen ten thousand times and will never be able to describe — the one that holds everything their life has been and makes it enough.
>
> "Thank you for being here."

> DESIGN: **The family ending is the Detective's never-returned ending.** A complete life. An unanswered question that stays unanswered because the detective decided some questions aren't worth what they cost. Lynn's words land with the weight of a person for whom speaking is physical labor — she spent effort on these words, which means they're the words she most needed to say. The game thanks the player for being mundane. For choosing the family. For closing the file. The warmth is real and the question underneath it is real and they coexist, and neither cancels the other.

### WEEK 3B — The rogue path: the descent

**DAYS 11-14:** Gerald goes back. On his own time. After hours. He's not assigned to the warehouse anymore — Tommy is found, the case is closed as far as his department is concerned. But he goes back.

He talks to Paul again — the manager, whose own denial cracked on the day Gerald looked into the hole. Paul is frightened and relieved in equal measure. He can get Gerald into the building after hours. He can't get him past the guard.

Gerald starts looking for another way down. Old building plans (incomplete — the same pattern as the property records). Utility access points. Storm drains. The infrastructure that exists beneath the surface of any old industrial district, which in this case connects to something that wasn't on any city plan.

**DAYS 14-16:** The Lynn texts are getting shorter. "Please call when you can." A missed dinner. A missed Sunday with Sarah. Gerald is home late, leaves early, is distracted when he's there. The marriage's daily rhythm is breaking down — not dramatically, not with a fight, just with absence. The warmth is still there. He's just not in the room enough to feel it.

**DAY 17 — The capture.**

Gerald pushes too far. The specifics of how — whether he finds an access point, whether Paul helps him, whether he's caught at the perimeter — can vary by what the player attempted. The result is the same: the institution collects him. Not violently. Professionally. A car. A ride. A facility he doesn't recognize. A room.

He waits.

---

**THE AUTHORITY FIGURE**

> The door opens and a man walks in carrying nothing — no file, no folder, no badge visible. He sits down across from Gerald the way Gerald has sat down across from a thousand people in this kind of room, and the symmetry is not lost on either of them.
>
> "Gerald." He knows the name. He also knows:
>
> "Your wife, Lynn — cerebral palsy, diagnosed at four, managed care through the county program and two private supplementals. Your daughter Sarah, married, expecting. Your mortgage is eleven months from paid off. You've been a detective for twenty-two years and you've never had a disciplinary action, which is genuinely unusual and speaks well of you."
>
> He lets that settle.
>
> "Here's what can happen. You go home. You take care of your wife. You work your cases. Everyone who knows you will continue to think you're the kind of man who finishes what he starts, because you are. Everything will be fine. Your daughter will have her baby. You'll be there for it. And you will never think about a warehouse, a hole, a container, or anything you saw or heard in the last three weeks again. That's option one."
>
> A pause.
>
> "Option two is you make this difficult. For yourself. For Lynn. For Sarah. For the baby."

Player choice:

- **Take the deal. Go home.** → Gerald nods. The man nods. It's over. → routes to the release
- **"Go to hell. I would never cooperate with this."** → routes to the refusal

**THE REFUSAL:**

> The man doesn't flinch. He doesn't get angry. He gets — if anything — slightly tired, the way a person gets tired when they've had this conversation before and it always ends the same way.
>
> "We win either way, Gerald. Look around you. Look at the world you live in. Your house. Your street. Your wife in her chair by the window. Do you know how good you have it? Do you have any idea how easily all of that could fall away?"
>
> He leans forward.
>
> "It is only through the day-and-night effort of people you will never meet, working in places you will never see, that the world stays as peaceful and as consistent as it is. That's not a threat. That's the truth. And your life — your mortgage, your cases, your badge — all of that is nothing. You are one man. You are one missing person. And if you like missing people so much — "
>
> He stops himself. Recalibrates. When he speaks again, the edge is gone.
>
> "What's going to happen is this. You're going to go home. You're going to take care of your wife. You're going to forget you ever saw anything to do with any warehouse. And you're going to move on with your life as a detective. That's not option one. That's what's going to happen."

> DESIGN: **The authority figure is the Operative corner introducing itself through the Detective's register.** His speech is the immune system's worldview delivered to someone it just swallowed: the world is maintained, the maintenance is invisible, and your life is a privilege of that maintenance, not a right. He knows about Lynn because the institution has been watching Gerald — his privacy is already gone, which means the investigation was never a surprise. They let him run until he crossed a line, and they knew exactly what leverage to use because they'd been reading his life the whole time. The player's choice (cooperate or refuse) changes the *texture* of the release — one Gerald walks out quietly, the other walks out with the authority figure's words ringing — but the outcome is identical. Gerald goes home. The institution wins either way.

**THE RELEASE**

> Gerald drives home in the dark. The road is the same road. The house is the same house. The porch light is on because Lynn always turns it on when he's late, and he's been late for weeks.
>
> He parks. He sits in the car for a minute. Then he goes inside, and the warm light and the hum of the house and the specific silence of a home where someone is waiting — all of it is exactly what it was before, and none of it is what it was before, and he will live inside that difference for the rest of his life.
>
> Lynn is in the living room. She looks up when he comes in, and whatever she sees on his face, she doesn't ask about it. She just looks at him the way she's looked at him for twenty years — the look that knows more than speaking could hold — and he sits down beside her and puts his hand on her shoulder, which is where his hand goes, and neither of them says anything.
>
> He'll work his cases. He'll be a good detective. He'll help his wife. He'll be there when the baby comes. And he will carry what he knows in the same place he carries Richmond — the quiet interior where the things he can't change and can't forget and can't speak about sit together in the dark, and the floor holds, because it has to, because there's nowhere else to stand.

> DESIGN: **The capture ending is the family ending's mirror.** Same house, same wife, same porch light, same hand on her shoulder. One detective chose this life. The other was sent to it. The words are almost identical. The man inside them is completely different. And the game doesn't tell the player which ending is better — because the family ending's Gerald is at peace with an unanswered question, and the capture ending's Gerald has the answer and can never use it, and both of those are real ways a life can go.

## §7 — The detective as future NPC

In either ending, Gerald is available for future stories as an NPC. His appearance depends on which ending the player's prior run produced:

**Family-ending Gerald:** A veteran detective. Solid. Good at his job. He gets assigned to help on a case that starts to rhyme with the warehouse, and at a certain point he goes quiet. He tells the new character: "Some cases close because there's nothing there. That's not a failure. That's the answer." He means it. He also means something underneath it that he'll never say.

**Capture-ending Gerald:** A veteran detective. Haunted. Still good at his job but there's something behind his eyes that wasn't there before. He gets close to the thread and then pulls back. He warns the new character to leave it alone. He won't explain why. If pressed, he changes the subject to Lynn, to Sarah's baby, to anything that isn't the warehouse. The player who played his story knows exactly why.
