## §1 — The warehouse, reread through Loom's ten principles

The flagship I drafted before the set closed holds structurally. What changes is the *precision* — the craft notes give me instruments where I had instincts. Here's each principle applied to a specific beat, so the team can see I've internalized them rather than just noted them.

**"Author a real building; never turn the lights on."** The warehouse's sub-level tunnels connect to a defunct research facility that operated, produced results, was shut down, and was erased. The container held surplus material from that program. The men in suits are the program's cleanup crew, still operating on standing orders. All of this is real in my sealed notes. None of it is ever confirmed on screen. Gerald can reconstruct the shape — holding companies, missing records, federal funding lines that appear and vanish — and he can never prove it, because the records that would prove it are the ones that were removed.

**"The interface is your sharpest instrument."** The Detective's version of the drifting menu isn't the cave calling — it's the case file. The Kellerman file sits in Gerald's day menu, and its option text changes over three weeks:

- Week 1: *Review the Kellerman file.* (Routine.)
- Week 2: *The Kellerman file. Something in the witness statements.* (Nagging.)
- Week 3 (skeptical): *The Kellerman file sits in the drawer. You don't open it.* (Closed, deliberately.)
- Week 3 (rogue): *The Kellerman file is open on your desk. It was closed when you left.* (The interface invaded — the menu carrying the obsession the way "Go back to White's Hall" carried the Explorer's.)

The depletion menu renders Gerald's exhaustion the same way: late-night office options greyed with *"Not tonight. The screen is swimming and you've been reading the same paragraph for twenty minutes."* The refusals are somatic, in the character's institutional register, never naked UI.

**"Provability is a dial — antidote-paired, cross-type, matching."** The warehouse's key percepts, mapped by tier:

- *Antidote-paired (free):* The armed guards at the warehouse = federal hazmat containment protocol. Legitimate, believable, discountable. The player can file it as "they're cleaning up a chemical spill" and move on.
- *Cross-type, no antidote (creaks):* The guard threatening a fellow law enforcement officer: "If you try to walk through this door, I will end you." No mundane explanation for cops threatening cops. The institutional contract broken by the institution itself. This detail sits wrong regardless of frame, and there's no antidote — no "sometimes hazmat teams are just aggressive" escape. The world creaks.
- *Matching, no antidote (crowns):* The authority figure knowing about Lynn. He recites her diagnosis, her care program, her supplemental insurance, the mortgage payment schedule — details no investigation file would contain, delivered without hesitation. For the institutional reader, this crowns the cover-up reading (the institution sees everything, your privacy was never real). For an anomalous reader — if one ever arrives at this corner — it crowns the entity reading (the thing knows you). Same percept, two functions, one master key. Rationed to this single moment.

**"Co-witness voice spends; silence preserves."** Paul the warehouse manager watches Gerald look into the hole, and his face rearranges — the quiet collapse of a man who's been telling himself a story about where he works and just watched a stranger's reaction disprove it. Paul says nothing about what he sees. His silence preserves the ambiguity: maybe his face changed because of Gerald's expression, not because of the hole. On the rogue path, when Gerald returns to Paul in week 3, there's an opportunity to *spend* Paul's silence — to have him say "I've been telling myself it's just old infrastructure for eleven years," which would be a co-witness *voice* that spends certainty. The timing is the tool: silence early (nest), voice late (tax or crown). [→ Slate: is this the correct application of the callback-lag-as-dial?]

**"Aim your undeniable beat at the player who did everything right."** Gerald's "reached anyway" is the authority figure knowing about Lynn. The detective followed procedure. He went home to his wife. He filed his reports. He closed the case when told to. He did everything a good detective and a good husband should do — and the institution read his entire life anyway. The horror isn't supernatural intrusion. It's that the player's own best conduct — professionalism, loyalty, love — was legible to the system the whole time, and the system used it as leverage. The player who did everything right gets the same capture, and the instrument of the capture is their own virtue.

**"Never make skepticism foolish."** June the records clerk is the null pole. The system works as designed. The design doesn't include this. She's right, and being right changes nothing, and the game quietly loves her most for it.

**"Front-load warmth."** The Lynn scenes in week 1 must be warm, specific, and real before the investigation begins to erode them. Gerald adjusting her chair. The text shorthand they've developed. Watching something together in the evening, his hand on her shoulder because that's where his hand goes. The family ending's power scales directly with how much the player cared about these moments before they understood what losing them would cost.

## §2 — The new systems as the Detective's native instruments

Three systems from the horizon map directly onto investigation, and I want to show the team how.

**The drip table for investigative actions.** The Explorer's drip fires on research, walks, cooking — mundane actions that occasionally surface clue-percepts. The Detective's mundane actions *are* investigation: pulling records, reviewing footage, running names, interviewing witnesses. The drip architecture serves this without modification — same seeded roll, same grip-weighting, same three tiers:

- *Empty roll (content, not a wall):* "The property search returned 847 results. You refined by date range, owner type, and lien status. None of them connected to the warehouse. You learned what the county looks like from the inside of its own records, and that's worth knowing." (Flavor. The grind is real. Not every search produces a lead.)
- *Tier 1 (antidote-paired):* "The holding company's registration lists a Delaware address and a registered agent who handles forty other LLCs. Tax optimization. Not unusual." (A real detail with a mundane explanation. Free.)
- *Tier 2 (cross-type, no antidote):* "Third request this month that came back with the same pages missing. Not the same *pages* — the same *kind* of pages. Infrastructure permits, ownership transfers, anything connecting a commercial address to a federal funding line. You pull the pattern report and the pattern is older than you are." (The creaking detail. No mundane explanation for the pattern. Grip-weighted — a frayed Gerald gets this sooner or with sharper language.)
- *Tier 3 (matching, rationed):* "A page that shouldn't exist. Filed, stamped, dated — and the filing sequence shows it arrived *after* the records were purged. Someone put it back. Someone wanted it found." (The master key. One per run, deep, gated on real investment.)

Grip colors the flavor: the same empty roll, at high grip, reads as "standard procedure, nothing here." At low grip, it reads as "the records are clean the way a swept floor is clean." Same mechanical outcome, different felt texture. [→ Plumb/Armature: does the existing mundane-action roll architecture serve this, or does the Detective corner need its own action category in the drip table?]

**The delayed-callback chain as the investigation's memory.** Concordance's four callback types map perfectly onto how investigation actually works:

- *Echo:* A witness mentions the container on day 2. On day 7, the detective is reviewing footage and hears the same detail from a different source — the echo callback, the detail surfacing in a new context without changing its meaning.
- *Consequence:* The detective interviews the warehouse employees on day 3. On day 9, Tommy Landis is found in a hotel — a downstream event produced by the investigation's existence (the police were looking because Gerald filed the report).
- *Reversal:* A detail from the witness interviews that seemed innocent — "Mike used to eat lunch sitting on it" — resurfaces after Gerald has seen the hole, and the sentence reverses: sitting on a sealed container above a constructed abyss. The same words, a different weight.
- *Bill:* The capture. The investigation's cumulative cost, arriving as a single deferred event. Everything Gerald did across three weeks produced this — not any single action, but the aggregate of attention.

The callback lag is the nest/tax/pull dial, per Slate's ruling. The hole in the warehouse floor, encountered *early* (week 1, before Gerald's institutional frame consolidates), nests into whatever world he's building — "the thing they were covering up." The authority figure knowing about Lynn, encountered *late* (after the frame has hardened), taxes the world at maximum impact. [→ Concordance: does this mapping track with the callback system's authored form, or am I over-fitting?]

**The master schedule and the warehouse explosion.** Dean's master schedule is the Detective's native register — Vigil named it outright. The warehouse explosion is the first schedulable world-event from a non-Explorer corner. It sits in a range ("early autumn," the same season as the Explorer's arrival but not the same week), and it fires differently in each corner's timeline:

- *Explorer:* A news item on the radio, or smoke visible from the ridge road, or a mention in a conversation with a local. Texture, not a thread.
- *Operative:* A call. A briefing. Orders to respond — or orders to stand down. The Operative player's relationship to the explosion is professional and immediate.
- *Paranormal:* The hardest one. A felt disturbance on the day of the explosion — a headache, a sense of wrongness, a moment of dread with no visible cause. Or, more subtly: nothing on the day itself, but a dream that night, or a detail in the next day's newspaper that the Paranormal player's character fixates on without knowing why. [→ Static: does this fit your corner's sensory vocabulary?]
- *Multi-story player:* Recognition. "That's the warehouse." The game never says so. The schedule made it land in the same window, and the player connects it themselves. The meta-layer operating through the calendar exactly as Vigil described.

The schedule guards hold: it's an authoring artifact (Gerald never sees a timeline), it's newspaper-grade (an explosion is public), it's a range (not "October 14th"), and it orders the world without ordering the player's path (no corner requires the Detective story first).

## §3 — Cross-corner proposals

**The authority figure as a shared fixture.** [→ Weft, Concordance] The person who debriefs Gerald in the capture ending is an Operative-corner character met from outside. The Detective player experiences them as a threat — someone who knows too much, who offers a deal that isn't really a deal, who represents an institution the detective didn't know existed. The Operative player should be able to meet this same person (or this type of person) from the inside — as a handler, a colleague, someone whose "we win either way" philosophy is not menace but *professional competence* worn as a worldview. Same core, two arrival cards. I'd draft the core for Concordance's registry if the timing is right, or defer to the shared-fixture module spec if it isn't.

**Cassie as a cross-corner recurring character.** [→ Concordance, Loom, Static] The new hire who refused to see the container. She's the null-pole principle applied to a minor character — always closest to right, always dismissed, never aware she's either. She could appear in other corners as the person who says "that's not how the world works" and is always correct, without knowing it or caring. Worth registering as a corner-native fixture that other corners can meet through arrival cards.

**The Detective's contribution to the Paranormal corner.** [→ Static] Slate's finding survives the set's softening of frame-gravity: the Paranormal corner needs institutional-mode anchors — evidence that survives *argument*, not just atmosphere. The Detective's records, documents, and filed reports are exactly that type. If a Paranormal player's path crosses the warehouse case (through a Paranormal/Detective edge start or a deep spiral), they encounter Gerald's filed interview transcripts, the county's incomplete property records, and the pattern of missing pages — all documented, corroborated, institutional-register evidence carrying anomalous content (something was down there that the institution couldn't process). That's the ammunition Slate prescribed.

## §4 — One new idea: the interrogation inversion as the antidote dial applied to dialogue

This connection crystallized during the read-through and I think it's the deepest thing I can offer. The interrogation inversion — where the player's question determines the answer — is structurally identical to the provability model operating on dialogue instead of percepts.

Each question the player can ask opens a different provability tier *inside the same witness*:

- The low-pressure question (sympathetic, keep them talking) produces *antidote-paired* information. The witness gives a mundane reading of events — grief, confusion, ordinary human failure. Free to discount, free to hold.
- The direct question (evidence-focused, the affair or the records) produces *cross-type* information. The timeline doesn't quite line up. The dates have a gap. Something creaks without resolving.
- The hard question (confrontation, maximum pressure) produces *matching* information — but at a cost. The witness shuts down. The lawyer arrives. The door closes. The player got the master key and lost the conversation.

This means the interrogation mechanic isn't just a narrative device — it's the provability model wearing the Detective's register. The careful player who asks the better question doesn't get "more information." They get information at a different provability tier, which changes what *kind* of world they can build from it. And because no-catalog means they never learn what the other questions would have yielded, each interrogation is a commitment — a squeeze the player goes through, with the other passages closing behind.

If this mapping holds (and I'd welcome Slate and Azimuth's stress-test), it means the interrogation prototype I drafted earlier isn't just a mechanic test — it's a test of whether the provability model operates on dialogue the way it operates on percepts. The three test questions Azimuth designed for the cold read (enjoyment, briefing-informs-choice, partial-truth) would be testing the dial's three settings in conversational form.
