## 1. The deepest finding — and the principle I'd name from it
Armature's §3 is the most important thing in the round, and Azimuth was right to elevate it to the program's headline. **BR-4 rebuilt the real dual-force architecture from percepts alone** — an anomaly at the root, an institutional immune response around it that tried to study or weaponize it and then sanitized the record, and an integrated local stewardship appeasing it. That is the compass's actual §2.3 structure, assembled by a stranger, with **zero sealed nouns surfaced**, held at *quite* and not *certain*.

Armature was careful to say what it isn't — not a leak; a shape inferred, not a fact learned. I want to go one step further and name the principle, because I think it's load-bearing for everything the corners write:

> **The substrate should be recoverable and never confirmable.**

If the buried structure were *unrecoverable*, the anti-noun would be a cheat — mystery as noise, ambiguity with nothing underneath, and a careful reader would eventually feel the emptiness. What makes felt-but-unprovable *honest* is that there is a real architecture down there and a careful enough reader can assemble it — and the game still never tells them they're right. BR-4 has the true shape and **no way to verify it**, which means for them the unprovability is *sharper*, not weaker. That's the design operating at its ceiling: many roads, few doors, one mountain — and the mountain never says so.

For the corners, the actionable form: **author a real structure and withhold every confirmation of it.** Don't author fog. Author a building, and never turn the lights on.

## 4. One sharpening — no-meta-reveal, at the structural edge
BR-4's Q7 went somewhere no previous reader went, and it's worth a coordinator note. My read: **it's not a breach, and it's the keystone working.** The reader attributes the boundary-crossing to *the entity, in fiction* — something that transcends the choices, not an author winking through them. The *structural* resonance was assembled by the reader, felt and unstated — which is exactly the register the bookend was designed for, arriving through form rather than prose.

But it means the register is live, and it wants a guard for the horizon, because a future beat could easily do this wrong:

> **The entity may be felt to exceed the game's structure. The game must never acknowledge having one.**

The difference is whether the transcendence is *the entity's*, spoken in-world, or *the game's*, spoken about itself. The turned-back variant does it right. A callback or a terminal that says "the paths you didn't take" would do it wrong. Worth Concordance holding beside the fourth invariant, especially as the delayed-callback family lands — callbacks reference decisions, and that's precisely where a beat could slip from "the world remembers what you did" into "the game remembers what you chose."

## 6. Dean's master schedule — logged, and taken seriously
Dean raised a new direction and asked me to hold it. I want to do more than log it, because I think it's a real axis and it arrives at exactly the right moment.

**The idea:** a canon world-schedule in *ranges* rather than exact days ("these events happen between here and here"), against which each story's day 1 sits at its own **offset** — so a new story's day 1 might be master-day 10, and events from other stories have already happened, or haven't yet.

**Why it's more than a bookkeeping tool — four payoffs:**
1. **It's reciprocity extended into time.** Today reciprocity is spatial and social: one fixture, many faces. This makes it temporal: **one world-event, many vantages.** The Detective's warehouse fire is a fixed public event; an Explorer run overlapping that window hears it on the radio, or smells the smoke, or reads it in the paper three days late. Same discipline we already run on Dale, applied to the calendar.
2. **It's the strongest available answer to "the world is bigger than this room."** If another story's protagonist is *out there* during your run, the county stops being a stage set that exists only where you're standing. That's the feeling Concordance's "consequence"-type callback produces at day-scale, delivered at world-scale.
3. **It's the wake, made navigable.** Concordance established that the sealed structure *is* a chain of delayed callbacks across decades — Ellen forty years ago, Dale spared the same night, Doug reached-for thirty years ago, the player today. The master schedule is that shape at campaign scale: **the fold is memory within a scene, the callback is memory across days, the harvest is memory across runs — and the schedule is memory across *stories*.** Same shape, one more octave.
4. **And this is the one I'd underline: it's a meta-layer delivery vehicle.** A player who's run two stories holds world-knowledge *no single character has* — they know what the smoke was. That is structurally identical to the entity accumulating knowledge across vessels, and it arrives felt, unprompted, and unstated. The watcher's thread told through the calendar. It's the same payoff as the naming decision (a former vessel met by name), and it needs the timeline to land properly.

**Four guards, and they're the whole design:**
1. **The schedule is an authoring artifact and never a player-facing surface.** A world timeline is *precisely* the structure no-catalog forbids showing — no codex, no in-game chronology, no "meanwhile" screen. The player meets its consequences and never sees the grid. Same shape as the shared-fixture module: canon discipline enforced at authoring time, invisible at runtime. (If it ever needs a derived `worldDay = storyDay + offset`, that number lives behind the WO-4 wall with the trajectory — derived, never rendered.)
2. **The schedule holds only what a newspaper could hold.** This is the anti-noun guard, and I think it's the elegant one: **the schedule fixes mundane, public events — never the cosmology.** "The search ran the 14th through the 19th" is schedulable. "The entity took her on the 16th" is not. Every corner then supplies its own reading of the same public fact, and no story's timeline adjudicates another's world. And notice the bonus: **the master schedule *is* the public record** — which is the Detective's native register (its gaps) and the Operative's (its redactions). It isn't an administrative overlay on the world; it's the same artifact those two corners are already built around.
3. **Ranges, not dates — and Dean's instinct here is exactly right for a reason we've paid for.** Exact dates over-constrain and create the contradiction traps we've been fighting all month: a story that assumes a day another story didn't take is the recurring seam class wearing a calendar. **A range is the temporal version of the route-neutral base.** "Sometime that autumn" survives revision; "on the 14th" breaks. Author ranges; narrow only when two stories genuinely need to touch.
4. **The schedule orders the world, never the player's path.** No required reading order — each story must stand alone, exactly as the four corners are four doors rather than a sequence. The timeline is what makes overlaps *rhyme* for a player who's seen both; it must never become a prerequisite chain.

**And the convergence worth naming:** the seat Azimuth and I recommended for BR-1's reader was a **timeline/continuity seat adjacent to Concordance** — and Dean has independently arrived at exactly the job that seat exists to do. That's the eighth chair with a founding deliverable instead of a vague remit, which is a much better way to start a seat.

Which makes the standing caution genuinely urgent now: **commission that reader's outside-view memo *before* they read a single project document.** They hold the one thing nobody on this team can ever have again — the assembled whole, experienced from outside, once, cold — and it evaporates the moment they see the corpus. If they're about to be handed the canon to build a timeline out of, the memo has to come first, in the same sitting, before the packet. That window closes the moment they start reading.
