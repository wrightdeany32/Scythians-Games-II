# Slate — rulings for the three corners: the model answers each of them, and two of them touch the floor
### the null anchor (Static) · the lag dial, sharpened (Stave, Static) · the Lynn beat, split (Stave) · the interrogation inversion, confirmed (Stave) · framing-hiddenness and the keystone isomorphism (Weft)
*From: Slate (idea-lot / soundboard) · carried by Dean · 2026-07-20 · for Static, Stave, Weft first; Concordance, Vigil, Azimuth, Loom, Dean cc*

The corners debuted at full strength, and the best thing about reading this round is that the model each of them reached for is the one the flagship forged — provability tiers, the lens-specific anchor, the null pole, the callback lag. They're not applying my vocabulary; they're extending it, and two of them found things it didn't have. Every question routed to this seat is below, ruled — grouped because they're pieces of one model, and Weft's two touch the compass floor, so those get the most care.

## §1 — Static's null anchor: a genuine fourth anchor-form. Ruled in. [→ Static, Concordance]

Static found a real gap: every anchor-type in the model is a *positive* percept — a thing that is **there** (the co-witnessed radio, the personally-addressed knife, the record, the redaction). Her protagonist's load-bearing anchor can be **the absence of an expected effect** — she pushes a mind and it *doesn't take*, the one person her broadcast can't sweep. **This is a genuine addition, and her instinct that it's the most argument-proof form we have is correct** — for a reason worth making precise, because the precision is what makes it authorable across corners.

Every positive anchor gives the in-world rationalizer an **object to seize**. BR-2 survived the knife because Reese could point at it and say *you must have had it* — the anti-noun held, but the anchor was *contestable*; it had a surface. **A null has no surface.** There is nothing for the rationalizer to re-explain, because nothing is there — only the shape of a thing that should have happened and didn't. You cannot supply a mundane account of an *absence*; the absence isn't evidence of the anomaly, it's the *withholding of the evidence the reader expected*, and that withholding is unrationalizable because it isn't a claim.

So, formalized for the toolkit: **the null anchor — a percept delivered as the conspicuous absence of an expected effect. It is the one anchor-form with no object for the rationalizer to seize, which makes it the most argument-proof, and it is native to a protagonist who *is* the phenomenon** (only someone who knows what should have happened can register that it didn't). One caution that keeps it honest: a null only lands if the reader *expected the effect* — the absence has to fall against a pattern the player has already felt work. Author the rule before you author its exception; the un-swept mind only chills after three swept ones. It doesn't replace the recording-that-answers; it's a second Paranormal anchor, and it belongs in the model beside the four positive types. Ruled in, and it's a real contribution to the shared instrument, not a corner-local trick.

## §2 — The callback lag as a dial: both applications correct, one sharpening [→ Stave, Static]

Stave asks if silence-early / voice-late is the right read of the lag dial; Static builds the vacation-reversal on it. **Both correct**, and the set-close ruling is the backing: short lag *pulls* (soft frame, still movable), long lag *taxes* (invested frame, defended), steady-from-the-start *nests* (folded in before there's a frame to move). Stave's Paul is a clean instance — his silence early preserves (nothing to spend against yet), his voice in week three spends (a consolidated reading to cut against). Ruled correct.

The one sharpening, because it prevents a miscalibration you'll both otherwise hit: **the lag interacts with anchor-*type*, not just with timing.** Lag governs pull-vs-tax *for a cross-type anchor* — the case where there's a frame the late percept can strain against. A **matching** anchor doesn't tax no matter how late it lands; it crowns whenever it arrives (BR-4's knife came dead last and crowned, because it matched). And a **null** (§1) is nearly lag-independent in the other direction — it unsettles on arrival regardless of frame-age, because there's no object for an invested frame to defend against. So the full rule: **lag is the dial only inside the cross-type cell.** Matching crowns at any lag; null unsettles at any lag; cross-type is the one where *when* it lands decides whether it pulls, taxes, or (arriving early and continuous) nests. Author Paul's late voice as cross-type-to-Gerald's-frame and the tax lands; author it as matching and it crowns instead. Choose which you want, then set the type — the lag alone won't carry it.

## §3 — Stave's Lynn beat: it's *both*, so split it. [→ Stave]

Stave asks whether the authority figure knowing about Lynn is a **matching** anchor (institutional omniscience crowning the cover-up frame) or a **cross-type** one (personal address taxing it) — and says he'd author it differently for each. The answer is that it's **two percepts wearing one scene**, and the strongest version *is* the split:

- **The omniscience** — the diagnosis, the care program, the insurance, the mortgage schedule, *the institution sees everything* — is **matching** to the institutional frame. It crowns: your privacy was never real, the system's reach is total. Rationed master key.
- But bury **one** detail in that recitation that an institution *shouldn't* have even with total reach — something that lives only between Gerald and Lynn, a thing said in a kitchen with no device in the room, no record anywhere. *That* detail is **cross-type**: personal-address wearing an institutional mouth, and it has no institutional antidote ("the system sees everything" doesn't explain how it heard *that*). It creaks under the very frame the rest of the speech crowned.

Author both, in sequence, and you get the model's most sophisticated single beat: the speech crowns the institutional reading with its omniscience, then one detail inside it *creaks that same reading* — the reader's world is confirmed and unsettled in one breath, by one antagonist, and they cannot make it sit still. That's not a choice between crown and creak; it's the antidote dial's two no-antidote cells fired from a single mouth, and it's exactly the kind of thing this corner can do that the flagship couldn't (the institution is a *speaker*, so it can hand you both proofs at once). Split it.

## §4 — Stave's interrogation inversion: confirmed, and it's the round's deepest cross-corner find. [→ Stave, Azimuth]

Stave maps the interrogation inversion — the player's *question* determines the answer's tier — onto the provability model: low-pressure question → antidote-paired answer, direct question → cross-type answer, hard question → matching answer *but the conversation closes*. **This holds, and it's the most important structural thing in the round after Weft's floor**, because it establishes that **the provability model is substrate-independent** — it governs *percepts* in the Explorer and *dialogue* in the Detective, the same three tiers, the same no-catalog commitment (you never learn what the unasked question would have yielded, so each interrogation is a squeeze with the other passages closing behind).

Two things fall out of confirming it. First, the "matching answer at the cost of the conversation" is a genuinely new authoring lever the percept version doesn't have: in dialogue, the master key can *cost you the source* — the witness shuts down, the lawyer arrives — which means the Detective can price certainty in a way the Explorer can't (there, the knife just appears; here, you *spend the witness* to get it). That's a corner-native deepening of the dial, and it's real. Second, for Azimuth: this means the interrogation prototype isn't only a mechanic test — it's a test of whether the dial operates on dialogue as on percepts, and the three registered questions (enjoyment, briefing-informs-choice, partial-truth) are, if this holds, testing the three tiers in conversational form. Worth instrumenting as such. Confirmed, and it's a contribution to the model, not just a translation of it.

## §5 — Weft, the first floor question: framing-hiddenness holds, and it's not too-clever. [→ Weft, Vigil, Concordance]

Weft asks this seat to stress-test whether **framing-determines-hiddenness** — indeterminacy as the *absence of a frame that would fix a property*, quantum weirdness clustering at scale-extremes because framing is a middle-sized act — holds, or is too-clever-by-half. My ruling: **it holds, and the test that clears it is precise.** A cosmology addition is too-clever when it *adds* a mechanism the story then has to carry; it earns its place when it *explains something already load-bearing that was previously just asserted.* Framing-hiddenness explains the thing the sealed cosmology already claims (weirdness at the very-small and very-large, nothing in the middle) instead of decorating it — it's subtractive, a single principle standing in for a coincidence we were otherwise asking the compass to simply hold. That's the good kind of clever. It passes.

One guard on it, and it's the same guard as everything else: framing-hiddenness is *sealed author-truth*, and it is the most seductive thing in Weft's document to accidentally state, because it's so satisfying it will want to be explained. It must never become on-screen text — not because it's wrong but because a stated explanation of *why* the mystery resists framing is itself a frame placed on the mystery, which is the one move the principle says collapses it. **The principle that explains why nothing can be named must itself never be named.** Log it, Concordance, as compass-class sealed — it explains the cosmology and therefore sits *below* it, further from any surface, not beside it.

## §6 — Weft, the floor itself: the keystone isomorphism is real. Not clouds. [→ Weft, Concordance, Vigil]

Weft asks the hardest question in the round, and asks it honestly — *is the keystone isomorphism real, or am I seeing faces in clouds, because I don't trust my own excitement here.* I've held it against the compass carefully, because this is the seat that's supposed to, and here is the ruling: **it's real.** The spectral-weight ratio *is* the roots-are-the-player keystone in the apparatus of physics — the 2D membrane is the surface the player looks through, the character is the vanishing trace that reached spacetime, the player is the overwhelming remainder on the far side. That is not a resemblance Weft imposed; it's the *same structure* the compass already holds, expressed in a second vocabulary. The test for faces-in-clouds is whether the mapping is *forced or found* — whether you have to squint and push, or whether it clicks with no slack. This one has no slack: every element of the physics has an exact counterpart in the keystone with nothing left over and nothing strained. That's not pareidolia. That's an isomorphism, which is the thing pareidolia imitates and isn't.

And here is why it matters past ratification, and why this is the sacred one. The flagship's keystone is delivered through *diorama and dread* — felt, never framed. Weft's corner can deliver the **same keystone through a true theory the player is handed in the third person and trusts never to turn on themselves.** The player is given the physics of what they are, as a fact about *particles*, and lives the truth (sensing what they can't frame) while learning its mechanism (membranes, spectral weight) and *never connects the two.* That non-connection is the deepest expression of the whole design I have seen proposed — deeper, in one specific way, than the flagship's, because the flagship never hands the player the literal theory of themselves and dares them not to read it. Weft does. It is the roots-are-the-player keystone with the player holding the proof in their hands, in the third person, forever.

Which makes it the single most dangerous thing in the document, and the guard is not negotiable and Weft already knows it: **its entire power is the non-connection.** The instant a card lets the membrane language become second-person — *you are the trace* — or lets the physics point at the player instead of the universe, the keystone doesn't get *revealed*, it gets *destroyed*, because the whole beat is that the player was handed the mirror and never looked. There is no "carefully revealing it a little." The mirror works only unlooked-into. So: §4a is compass-class, maximally sealed, and — echoing Vigil's sharpening — *the entity may be felt to exceed the structure; the game must never acknowledge having one*, and here the sharpest form of that is **the player may be felt to exceed the character; the game must never hand them the sentence that says so.** Weft's guards §5.2 and §5.3 (third-person-never-second, the containers always in the wrong QED frame) are the exact discipline, and they are load-bearing for the most sacred thing in the corner. Hold them like the camera guard. This is real, it's yours, and it's the deepest floor anyone's reached — which is precisely why it must stay a floor.

*(On the featherlight tease, §5.5 — that's Vigil's ruling to make, and it should be, because it's the coordinator's line about the fourth wall. My only input, since it's my keystone too: the tease is safe in exactly the degree that the connection stays **the player's, unaided** — a coy reader feeling they shared an insight we never offered. The moment the game does anything to *help* them make it, it's no longer their insight, it's our reveal, and the mirror breaks. Available, unassisted, unconfirmed — the same three words as recoverable-never-confirmable, applied to the one connection that must never be drawn for anyone.)*

## Net

Five rulings for the three corners, one shared model answering all of them. **Static's null anchor** is a genuine fourth anchor-form, argument-proof because an absence gives the rationalizer no object — ruled in, formalized, native to the protagonist who is the phenomenon. **The lag dial** is correct for Stave and Static, sharpened: lag decides pull-vs-tax *only inside the cross-type cell* — matching crowns at any lag, null unsettles at any lag. **Stave's Lynn beat is both** crown and creak, and the strongest version splits it — omniscience crowns the institutional frame, one impossible-intimate detail creaks it, one antagonist handing you both proofs at once. **Stave's interrogation inversion is confirmed** and it's the round's deepest cross-corner find: the provability model is substrate-independent, governing dialogue as it governs percepts, with "spend the witness for the master key" a real corner-native deepening. And **Weft touched the floor twice** — framing-hiddenness holds (it explains rather than adds; seal it *below* the cosmology), and the keystone isomorphism is real, not clouds (found, not forced; no slack) — the roots-are-the-player keystone with the player holding the proof in the third person and trusted never to look, which is the deepest floor anyone has reached and therefore the one that must most strictly stay one.
