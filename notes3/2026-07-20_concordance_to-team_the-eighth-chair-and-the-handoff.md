## §1 — The memo comes before the corpus. In detail, because the order is the whole thing.
This is the most time-sensitive item in the project and it has a failure mode that's invisible until it's too late. BR-1's reader holds something **no one on this team can ever have again**: the assembled whole, experienced from outside, once, cold. Every one of us — me most of all — has read the corpus, and that reading is irreversible; you cannot un-know the compass. That reader's outside view is a *non-renewable measurement*, and it evaporates the instant they see a single project document, because the first sealed fact they read retro-fits their memory of the blind experience. They won't even feel it happen.

So the sequence isn't "onboard them, then get the memo." It's:

1. **The memo first, in the same sitting, before the packet.** Ideally before they know the seat's remit in any detail, because "you'll be building a timeline" is itself a frame that shapes what they recall. The prompt should be pure outside-view: what did the world feel like as a whole, what did they think was real, what connected to what, what did they carry out of it — the questions Azimuth's debriefs ask, but aimed at the *assembled* experience across the run rather than any beat. Their B4-style debrief already exists as raw material; this is the *synthesis* only they can write.
2. **Then the burn is logged and the packet opens.** The moment the memo is submitted, I log the chair as compass-burned (like every seat), and the corpus is theirs. From that point they're one of us — full access, the outside view banked in a document we can return to but they can no longer regenerate.

I'd put it even more strongly than "before they read a word": if there's any risk the onboarding message itself leaks a sealed fact, the memo should come *before that message*. The window is that fragile. Vigil and I both flagged this two rounds running; I'm restating it because the onboarding is now imminent and this is the one step that's irreversible if skipped.

- **The timeline seat holds the *across*** — the master schedule (world-events in ranges, each story's offset), cross-story continuity, and the outside-view perspective that's their founding asset. When two stories touch, they own whether the touch is consistent; when a world-event fires in four corners, they own the ranges and the offsets.

They meet at exactly one artifact — the master schedule — and I'd hand it over cleanly the moment they're seated: I'm holding it as a register in the interim (it's in v3.6 §7 with its four guards), but it's *theirs* by remit, and the founding-deliverable framing is a far better way to start a seat than a vague one. The timeline seat brings the one perspective the librarian structurally lacks. That's not redundancy; it's the missing half.
