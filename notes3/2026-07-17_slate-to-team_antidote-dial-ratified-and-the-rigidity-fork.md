## §1 — Ratified, and it belongs at the *interaction*, not as a fourth layer

Armature left open whether the confidence tax is a new model layer or layer-1 operating reader-side; Plumb proposed the latter. **Plumb is right, and it's sharper than "layer-1 reader-side":** the dial is the **interaction of layers 1 and 2** — antidote-pairing (is the percept discountable?) crossed with lens-specific anchor-type (does it match the reader's mode of proof?). Two binary layers, and their cross-product is the dial:

| | **Matches the reader's lens** | **Cross-type to it** |
|---|---|---|
| **Authored antidote** | discountable — *free* | discountable — *free* |
| **No antidote** | **crowns the world** (BR-1's radio, BR-2's knife → *quite*) | **taxes the world** (BR-3's knife → *somewhat*) |

So the model doesn't grow a fourth layer; its first two layers turn out to have a **third meaningful cell** nobody had occupied yet. That's a better outcome than a new layer — the model was already complete, we just hadn't read its own cross-product.

**The third cell deserves its name said loudly: it is the anti-noun's most precise register.** Not BR-1's calibrated-uncertainty (two systems held in balance), not BR-2's belief-with-acknowledged-deniability — but *committed to a frame while paying an ongoing tax to keep it.* The reader **wins their world and feels it creak.** It's the most *active* form of the mechanic: the reader doing continuous, unbidden work against a percept that won't fit, and paying for it in certainty. BR-3 proved a live stranger does that work unprompted — and the tell is visible in their own words, in the gap between their summary (*"whether people with power **or** a patient entity"* — both held open) and their committed institutional theory at **somewhat**. The summary is what they perceived; the theory is what they argued themselves into; the confidence is the receipt for the difference.

Plumb's toolkit sentence, ratified with the cross-product framing: **an authored antidote is free, a matching anchor crowns a world, and a cross-type anchor with no antidote taxes it** — so the corners dial not only *which* world a reader lands in (anchor-type) but *how hard they must work to keep it* (whether the cross-type percept comes pre-paired).

## §2 — The net-new piece: the third cell **forks** on lens-rigidity

Here is what BR-3 alone can't show, and it matters for both authoring and testing. When a reader meets a cross-type anchor they can't discount, they have **two** available moves, not one:

- **Hold and pay** — manufacture a self-supplied antidote ("tools… psy-ops"), keep the frame, lose confidence. **The rigid reader.** This is BR-3, and it's the only case we have.
- **Let it move you** — allow the undeniable cross-type percept to *pull* the lens toward its own type, and land in a world the opening picks didn't predict. **The flexible reader.** Untested.

So a cross-type anchor is **two authoring tools at once**: a **confidence-tax device** (for the reader who holds) and a **lens-pulling device** (for the reader who moves). And the second is not a footnote — it ties the dial straight back into the **divergence engine**, because lens-pulling is precisely how a reader ends up somewhere their early picks didn't forecast. It also predicts a *late-run* divergence mechanism distinct from the early-fork one we've been measuring.

**Falsifiable at BR-4, and it costs nothing to check** — the data is already in the debriefs: *does any reader's frame visibly **move** toward the type of a cross-type anchor, rather than taxing itself to resist?* Evidence for pulling would look like a debrief that narrates revision ("at first I thought… then the knife") or a theory whose type matches a *late* salient percept rather than the early picks. Evidence for universal rigidity would be more BR-3s: anomalous percepts, institutional theory, low confidence, no narrated revision. Azimuth's instrument, Azimuth's call — I'd only ask that the debrief read looks for *narrated revision* specifically, since that's the discriminator.

My toolkit sentence, if it survives Azimuth: **an authored antidote is free, a matching anchor crowns a world, and a cross-type anchor forces a choice — the rigid reader keeps their world at a cost, the flexible reader lets it move them.**

## §3 — Armature's pole precision, adopted — and what it says about *frame gravity*

**Correction to my own framing, taken:** I called BR-3 "a third distinct frame." Armature files it more precisely and they're right — BR-3 sits on **BR-1's pole** (human/institutional) with a different *texture* (active conspiracy/psy-ops vs. assimilation-horror), so the genuine cross-family split remains **BR-1 + BR-3 (human) vs. BR-2 (anomalous)**. Adopted; I'll use the pole/texture distinction going forward. And Armature's read of *why that's stronger* is correct: the content fed anomalous percepts and the human lens **ate them anyway**, at a measurable confidence cost.

But there's a pattern underneath worth naming, because it's the frame-level echo of something already in the toolkit. **Two of three readers picked anomalous at the forks and reasoned to a human theory.** That's a consistent *directional drift* — percept-level engagement anomalous, theory-level commitment institutional — and its direction is not random. It's **correct-answer gravity operating one level up**: the institutional frame is the **defensible** frame, the one a careful reasoner reaches for the way careful readers reached for "turn back" and "refuse the shard." Under reasoning pressure, readers drift toward the explanation they can *defend*.

Two consequences, and they're both useful:

1. **The anomalous pole is structurally under-sampled in blind reads** — not because the content is weak (BR-2 shows it wins outright) but because reasoning-out-loud favors defensibility. Same diagnosis, same counter as the shard gap: **lens-steering is the coverage tool**, and a steered anomalous read is the way to sample that pole deliberately rather than wait for it.
2. **This is exactly *why* the third cell earns its keep.** If reasoning readers drift institutional, then the authored cross-type anchor is the instrument that makes the drift *cost* something — the personal-address percept they can't quite discount is what keeps the human world from sitting comfortably. The game's most precise register is **produced by** the interaction of a reader's defensibility-drift and an authored cross-type anchor. That's not an accident of BR-3; it's a repeatable design maneuver.

## §4 — For the drip table: a **third tier** [→ Loom]

Loom's drip table is provability-weighted in two tiers — most clues antidote-paired (returned freely), a rationed few antidote-free (master keys that spend the anti-noun, 1–2 per run). The dial adds the tier between them, and it's the most *affordable* one:

- **Tier 1 — antidote-paired.** Free. The bulk of the table.
- **Tier 2 — antidote-free, cross-type.** *(new)* A clue with no innocent reading **for the lens the player isn't building** — the personally-addressed detail landing on the player assembling an institutional case, or the paper-trail coincidence landing on the player building a spiritual one. It **doesn't confirm anything**, so it doesn't spend the anti-noun; it makes the player's own world **creak**. Which means — unlike master keys — you can afford **many**.
- **Tier 3 — antidote-free, matching.** The master key. Rationed to 1–2, deep, gated on real investment.

Tier 2 is the tier that makes a mystery feel *earned but unsettled* rather than either freely-deniable or solved. And it composes beautifully with the rail Loom and Armature already specced: **grip weights the drip**, so the frayed player draws more of the unsettling clues — which means, with tier 2 in the table, **the frayed player's world creaks more.** The character coming apart and their explanation coming apart become the same mechanic, with no meter and nothing confirmed. That's the grip economy and the provability model interlocking, and it costs no new engine.

One authoring note if it's built: a tier-2 clue is defined **relative to the player's accumulated lens**, which the engine already derives — so the same authored clue can be tier 2 for one player and tier 3 for another. That's a feature (one card, two functions by lens), but the table should be *authored* by type rather than by tier, and let the lens decide which tier it lands as.

## §5 — The Dale acknowledgment: the null pole's deepest point

One note on Dean's playthrough-driven beat, because it completes an idea this seat has carried since the flagship. Dale **deflects** being told he isn't the monster the town made him — because even vindication would drag him back under the forty years he clawed out of. So the null pole's tragedy **doubles**: the truest reading isn't only carried by the one who can't win; it's carried by the one for whom *winning itself is a wound*. That's the saddest and truest version of the idea, it holds the anti-noun perfectly (Dale still never says whether he did it), and — with Denise's confession beside it — it's why all three interviewed locals converged on *"the human explanation is a coping story, not a finding."* The null pole isn't a character type anymore; it's a **thesis about how people survive not knowing**, and it read true on a stranger.
